able to adapt to Tokyo.  
 
  
 
Over the next two weeks of my school trip, I stayed with a host family and went to school in 
Saitama. During my time there, the other students and I were encouraged to explore the city 
whenever we had free time, which is exactly what we did.   
 
  
 
One day after school, I guided the entire group of students from school in Saitama to Akihabara, 
the anime capital of the world. This excursion was my first time going out on my own without my 
host family. As we took the famously complex train lines to Akihabara, my palms started to sweat 
because of how nervous I was. After we arrived, I made sure everyone got off the train and out of 
the train station.  
 
  
 
Once we left the station, I was in awe. I walked to the main street and went from building to 
building trying new food, buying souvenirs, and talking to random people.  From the end of the 
school day until around 10:30 that night, I roamed the streets and experienced the amazing culture 
surrounding me.   
 
  
 
Akihabara felt like one giant sensory overload: neon lights, clashing sounds, and a language I 
could barely understand came from all angles. All the distractions seemed to add to the confusion 
I felt at the beginning of the trip, but I also felt pulled in towards the city.

buildings, and swarms of people seemed to trap us in the streets of Akihabara. I felt like an ant 
wandering in a maze, with no clear way out. No one could help me, and I felt isolated, even among 
my peers.
