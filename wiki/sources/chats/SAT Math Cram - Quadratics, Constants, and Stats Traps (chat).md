# SAT Math Cram - Quadratics, Constants, and Stats Traps

The night before an SAT sitting (2025-09-30), [[Traveler Stansberry]] used ChatGPT (GPT-5) as a last-minute math tutor. The chat is mislabeled "Scam alert analysis" only because it opened with an unrelated question: he screenshotted a smishing text impersonating the Maryland MVA (a +63 Philippines number, fake `md.payxiit.cc` link, urgency threats) and asked "is this real" — the model walked through the five tells that flag it as a scam. He then immediately pivoted to feeding the model photographed practice problems.

## What he was actually doing
This is a pure cram-mode tutoring session. Traveler already holds a **740 in math** and explicitly told the model he didn't need to *do* problems — he just wanted to *recognize structure* fast enough to convert the last few "curve-breaker" questions into a 760+. He worked through a stack of photographed digital-SAT-style problems, each answered with a step-by-step boxed solution:

- **Graph translations** — translating `9x − 10y = 19` down 4 units (sub `y → y+4`), finding the new x-intercept.
- **Matching a quadratic to a table** of values by plug-and-eliminate.
- **Perpendicular slopes** (negative reciprocal).
- **Density/mass** of a cube (mass = density × volume).
- **No-solution linear equations** — coefficients of x equal, constants differ.
- **Discount traps** — "80% off" vs "80% of" (a classic misread he had gotten wrong).
- **Discriminant / no-real-solutions** thresholds.
- **Integer-divisibility factoring** — `(hx+k)(x+j)` forcing `45/k` to be an integer.
- **Rational functions** — pinning a numerator from graph coordinates (he pushed back: "how'd you know the numerator was 6").
- **Vieta's formulas** — sum/product of roots, including a `kab` product problem.
- **Completing the square** for a circle equation (he asked "y do −1/4", probing the mechanics rather than accepting the answer).
- **Vertex form** and `a+b+c` reasoning.

## Concept reviews he requested
After the photo problems he asked for structured rundowns of the higher-level topics he expected to see "tomorrow": all **statistics traps** (mean vs median sensitivity to outliers, SD under shift vs scale, boxplots/percentiles), **advanced probability** (complement rule for "at least one," dependent draws without replacement, conditional probability, expected value), **advanced problem types** (complex-number conjugate division, function transformations, piecewise, logs/exponentials, sequences, nonlinear systems), **quadratic graphing** across vertex/factored/standard forms, and especially **"mystery constant" problems** — the "alphabet soup" of unknown coefficients that he said trips him up most. He had the model demystify "always positive/negative" quadratics via the discriminant-and-leading-coefficient grid and the equivalent vertex/`k = −D/4a` method.

## What it shows
A revealing snapshot of how Traveler studies under time pressure: he optimizes for **pattern recognition over grinding**, repeatedly asks *why* a step works ("y do −1/4", "how'd you know the numerator was 6") rather than just banking answers, and self-assesses honestly ("this stuff always gets me tripped up the most"). It fits his quantitative trajectory toward [[UVA and the Quant Question]] and a finance/math path, and complements his in-school [[IB Math (SL)]] work — the SAT here is the standardized-testing layer of his [[Academic Record]] and [[College Search]] prep. The opening scam-detection question also shows his instinct to verify before acting.
