## The Salesman essay (the real work)
Traveler supplies his own prompt and thesis: *How does Arthur Miller question traditional masculine identity through character development?* His thesis ties the [[Masculinity|identity of American men]] to "ethical values, idea of success, and work ethic," arguing the collapse of identity aligns with the role of father figures. This is squarely his recurring interest in [[Identity and Its Collapse]] and [[Masculinity]] — and it overlaps thematically with his other curriculum texts on hollow [[Consumerism and Alienation|American success myths]] like [[The Great Gatsby]].

He pulls three lines of material from the model:
- **Character development** — Willy as both victim and architect, defining manhood through being the breadwinner; Biff's arc as rebellion against his father's "toxic model"; Happy as the cautionary echo who reproduces the system unreflectively.
- **Setting** — Brooklyn house "hemmed in" by apartment buildings as a symbol of Willy's trapped mind; the transparent walls during flashbacks as psychological realism; the city as a machine that "promised freedom but built walls."
- **Quotes on success** — "Be liked and you will never want," "I'm vital in New England," Ben's "The jungle is dark but full of diamonds," and Biff's Requiem line "He had all the wrong dreams." The model groups them by sub-theme (surface masculinity, identity-through-work, toxic ideals, Biff's counterpoint) to mirror his thesis structure.

The framing — masculinity built on external validation rather than integrity, the father-son transmission of false ideals — is the analytical spine he reuses across his English work.
