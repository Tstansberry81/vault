# Economics EE Four Arguments and BLS-FRED Data Sourcing

A long, high-stakes working session (titled "Response modes options") in which Traveler drafts, restructures, and sources his [[Extended Essay (Economics)]] — the COVID-19 fiscal/monetary policy → inflation paper grounded in Maryland (the Baltimore-Columbia-Towson CPI region). The essay is due the following Monday at full length (4,000 words plus graphs), and he treats the chat as a live editing partner, pasting paragraph after paragraph for review and demanding "accurate and credible" data with links. This is one of the most substantive [[IB Economics (SL)]] artifacts in the vault, showing the EE near completion.

## The "response modes" framing
The chat opens with a custom ChatGPT persona menu Traveler had set up: **0 = Normal** (cynic/witty), **1 = Researcher**, **2 = Data Analyst** (must validate assumptions), **3 = Advice Giver**, **4 = Coder** (for [[Homework Hatch (startup)]]). ## The thesis and four arguments
Working thesis (final form): *"Coronavirus' reactionary fiscal and monetary policy caused significant inflation in 2022 and minor inflation in 2021 and 2023 due to the excessive increase in aggregate demand, increasing the debt and decreasing interest rates, rising energy prices, and tightening the labor market, which affected price inflation in Maryland."* The four body arguments, refined over the session:

1. **Demand-pull from stimulus** — CARES Act (~$270B relief, part of ~$4 trillion injected) and ARPA (~$400B) flooded aggregate demand into a supply-constrained economy (cites Goodwin et al., Pandemic Oversight).
2. **Debt + interest rates** — national debt rose >$4T in 2019–2020; the federal funds rate was cut to ~0; he debates whether to combine debt and interest rates into one argument and learns the federal funds rate is monetary (not fiscal) policy, that it's an overnight inter-bank rate tied to reserve requirements, and that "debt inflation" is acceptable terminology.
3. **Rising energy/gas prices** — gas peaked ~$5/gal in Maryland June 2022; he wrestles with whether mentioning the Russia-Ukraine war strays from his federal-policy thesis, ultimately leaning on the MIT Sloan finding (Kritzman/Vereckey) that ~42% of inflation was federal-spending-driven to argue energy was downstream of policy. Adds an OPC source showing low-income Baltimore/Eastern Shore households spend ~15% of income on energy.
4. **Labor market tightness / discouraged workers** — stimulus disincentivized work; he tests and then largely abandons a sub-argument that Maryland's minimum-wage hike ($13.25→$15) re-incentivized labor, after FRED data shows the **labor participation rate** actually *fell* (65.3% end-2023 → 64.7% July 2025), never recovering to pre-COVID levels.

## Data and method
He sourced hard numbers and graphs: national CPI-U 260.474 (Dec 2020) → 305.109 (June 2022 peak); Maryland CPI-U 261.535 → 306.506 (~6.3% vs ~6.5% national); 2023 cooling to 3.4% national / 2.1% Maryland; federal funds rate time series from FRED; Maryland debt figures (the $4.87B question) from the CDAC report. He clarifies methodology points with the model: the 4,000-word EE limit **does** count in-text citations but not the bibliography; "Maryland" is operationalized as the Baltimore-Columbia-Towson metro; and the IB does require an annotated bibliography. He also pushes back on the model — rejecting unrequested rewrites ("i didnt want revisions i wanted suggestions"), cutting an EIA source that "weakens my argument," and policing repeated evidence that makes the case "look weak."

## What it reveals
This is Traveler at his most rigorous and self-correcting on coursework: he stress-tests his own arguments (killing the minimum-wage angle when the data contradicts it), distinguishes correlation from causation, and keeps the essay disciplined around a single federal-policy thesis.
