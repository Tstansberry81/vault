# New Liberalism, Keynesianism, and the Progressive Policy Institute

A short self-directed political-theory walkthrough from December 2025 in which [[Traveler Stansberry]] asks ChatGPT to unpack **new liberalism** (social liberalism) — the model needles him for "studying political theory for fun." It reads as enrichment adjacent to [[IB History (HL)]] and [[IB Economics (SL)]] rather than a specific assignment, and it maps cleanly onto his [[Political and Economic Views]] and [[Intellectual Interests]].

## What he learned
- **New liberalism** (late 19th–early 20th c.) reframes freedom from classical liberalism's "absence of interference" to having the **capacity and resources** to act — "positive freedom." Key thinkers: **T. H. Green** (positive liberty), **L. T. Hobhouse** (*Liberalism*, 1911; wealth comes from society so society may regulate it), **J. A. Hobson** (poverty as systemic, critic of imperialism). It supplied the philosophical backbone of the modern welfare state: compulsory schooling, workplace safety, unemployment insurance, pensions, public health, monopoly regulation.
- He probes: "so like keynesian economics sort of." The model distinguishes them — new liberalism is a **political philosophy** (social justice + real freedom), Keynesianism (1930s) is an **economic theory** (fiscal intervention for macro stability). They share the rejection of market self-correction but aren't identical: "Keynes himself wasn't trying to build a welfare state; he was trying to stop capitalism from eating itself."

## The Progressive Policy Institute
He then pivots to the **Progressive Policy Institute (PPI)**, a D.C. think tank founded 1989 by the Democratic Leadership Council as the "idea mill" for "New Democrats." Center-left but pro-market/pro-innovation — "radically pragmatic," a *better* government rather than a *bigger* one. In 2020 it absorbed the Neoliberal Project as its **Center for New Liberalism**. He asks specifically about **funding**: a 501(c)(3) with ~$9.17M revenue (2023), backed by foundation grants and corporate sponsors (Walton Family Foundation, Fidelity Charitable, Big Tent Project Fund), with noted transparency concerns about Big Tech donors influencing policy output.

## What it reveals
The funding question — and his instinct to treat think-tank research "as potentially influenced by donor interest" — fits his skeptical, follow-the-incentives temperament also visible in his finance work and his determinist, [[Individual vs. Society]] leanings. The chat shows him triangulating the ideological lineage from classical liberalism through new liberalism to "third way" centrism, the same terrain his [[Extended Essay (Economics)]] and Econ coursework keep circling (fiscal policy, government intervention, market failure).
