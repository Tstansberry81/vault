# IB HL History Exam Cram - Study Guide, Term Sheet, and Post-Exam Debrief

A sprawling, days-long cram session in the run-up to (and immediately after) Traveler's [[IB History (HL)]] final exams in May 2026. The chat is effectively three things stacked together: a generated study packet, a tutoring walkthrough of all three papers, and a candid post-exam debrief of how he actually answered.

## Building the study guide
He opened by dumping "a tremendous amount" of material — full textbook chapters as PDFs plus a zip of review docs — and asked ChatGPT to build a paper-by-paper study guide covering his chosen options: **Paper 1 — Rights and Protest**; **Paper 2 — Authoritarian States, Causes and Effects of 20th-Century Wars, and Cold War superpower tensions**; **Paper 3 — Americas sections 12, 13, 14, 16, 17**. Over several iterations he had it expanded "for people who haven't paid attention at all in class," merged into one combined document, folded in his own class notes and warm-up drills (with corrections to sloppy class shorthand), and produced a one-line-bullet **term sheet for Quizlet flashcard import** spanning every unit. This is the same teach-others, build-the-tool-once instinct behind [[Homework Hatch (startup)]] and [[Coding Club]].

## Tutoring walkthrough
He then had it run him through each paper conversationally. Paper 1: the four-question structure (message, message+inference, compare/contrast, OPCVL value-and-limitations, and the sources-plus-own-knowledge Q4), with timing splits and the rule to "read like a lawyer, write like a sniper." Deep dives on **African American civil rights** (Plessy, Brown, Montgomery, sit-ins, Freedom Rides, Birmingham, Selma, CRA 1964 / VRA 1965, NAACP/SCLC/SNCC/CORE, MLK vs Malcolm X, Black Power) and **youth protest** (SDS, Free Speech Movement, antiwar, counterculture).

Paper 2 work focused on picking case studies: he settled on **Mao, Castro, and Stalin** as his authoritarian leaders, and **Korea and Vietnam** plus a non-Asian war (the **Spanish Civil War**) for the 20th-century wars topic. Extensive content on Stalin's rise and consolidation, the **Stalin vs Mao vs Deng** comparison, the Sino-Soviet split / why Mao and Khrushchev clashed, and the chain of US Cold War presidents (Truman through Reagan) with their doctrines. Paper 3 prep leaned into his self-identified strengths — Cold War conflicts, presidential policy, civil rights — with backup material on the **Great Depression and the New Deal** (TVA, PWA, WPA, Hoover vs FDR) in case he drew "some ass essay questions."

## Post-exam debrief
After sitting the papers he reported back. Paper 1 was a source set on the **Freedom Rides** — he "cooked" the prescribed-topic source work but admitted a factual slip (he wrote that Kennedy sent the National Guard to Little Rock; it was Eisenhower) while arguing that nonviolent protest's [[Civil Disobedience]] component forced administrations to act. His Paper 2 essays: Stalin vs Mao on "authoritarian states emerging due to propaganda" (he argued Stalin leaned on **fear** more than propaganda — and conceded he wrongly wrote "KGB" instead of NKVD), and Korea vs Vietnam on whether war outcomes were "determined by the mobilization of human and economic resources" (he argued mobilization barely mattered — Korea stalemated because Chinese intervention followed Inchon, and Vietnam turned on public approval and Vietnamese fighting spirit).

## What it reveals
A snapshot of Traveler under real exam pressure: strategic about playing to strengths, fluent enough in the content to build his own teaching materials, but human in the small factual misfires he frets over afterward. The fear-vs-propaganda and resources-don't-determine-outcomes arguments show a contrarian, causal-mechanism habit of mind consistent with his [[Political and Economic Views]] and his determinism noted in [[Traveler Stansberry]].
