# Biden vs Trump Corporate Tax, Deregulation, and a Regulatory Certification Idea

A January 2026 ChatGPT (GPT-5) session in which Traveler researches U.S. corporate tax and deregulation policy, then floats an original policy-design idea of his own before the chat decays into Maryland-geography trivia. The work reads like prep for an [[IB Economics (SL)]] macro/policy discussion or an advisor-board/club exercise (it echoes the framing of his "Trump Corporate Tax Policy Analysis - Advisor Board Project" chat), with the assistant repeatedly offering "IB-safe evaluation sentences" and stakeholder/opportunity-cost breakdowns.

## What he asked and learned
- **Biden corporate tax:** the headline rate stayed at 21% (TCJA level); Biden's proposed 28% hike never passed. The real changes were the **15% Corporate Alternative Minimum Tax (CAMT)** on book income for firms with $1B+ profits, a **1% stock-buyback excise tax** (later 4%), support for the **OECD 15% global minimum tax**, and a large IRS enforcement funding boost. Framed as "patching the leaks Trump left behind" rather than reversing TCJA.
- **Trump's response (2025 "One Big Beautiful Bill Act"):** locks the 21% rate in permanently, makes bonus depreciation permanent, expands the 20% pass-through deduction, eases CAMT anti-avoidance and buyback rules via guidance, and softens Biden's international tightening. Net effect: lower effective corporate taxes and stronger investment incentives, but larger deficits and less progressivity.
- **80% bonus depreciation:** he asked what it implies and got the mechanics — immediate first-year deduction of 80% of a capital asset's cost (e.g. $800k on a $1M machine → ~$168k upfront tax savings at 21%), which affects the *timing* not the *total* of taxation, pulls investment forward, lowers effective (not statutory) rates, and signals a phase-out schedule (100→80→60→40→20→0).
- **Deregulation proposals:** one-in-two-out rule, energy/EPA rollbacks, Dodd-Frank financial deregulation, labor/contractor classification loosening, healthcare/FDA speed-ups, and the tariff contradiction (deregulate at home, protectionism abroad).
- **"Skip-regulation badge?"** — no magic pass exists, but firms use thresholds/exemptions, ISO-style certifications, regulatory sandboxes, waivers/no-action letters, federal preemption, and SBA carve-outs.

## His own policy idea
The most revealing turn: Traveler proposes a **hypothetical "once-certified, mostly-trusted" regime** — a firm passes one rigorous sector check and earns a ~10-year pass on further regulation in that sector. The assistant takes it seriously, naming the failure modes (moral hazard, regulatory capture by incumbents, sector heterogeneity) and refining it into a **Tiered Regulatory Certification System (TRCS)**: modular per-domain passes, 5–10 year horizons scaled to risk, light-touch random audits, and instant revocation triggers — with smaller firms getting cheaper/faster certification to protect competition. This shows his characteristic move of generating a concrete systems-design proposal and pressure-testing it, consistent with his [[Political and Economic Views]] (market-friendly but attentive to externalities and capture) and his [[Intellectual Interests]] in mechanism design.
