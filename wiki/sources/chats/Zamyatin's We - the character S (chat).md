# Zamyatin's *We* — the character S (chat)

A short July 2025 chat in which Traveler interrogates a single character from **Yevgeny Zamyatin's dystopian novel *We*** — the Guardian known only as **"S"** — followed by a brief unrelated tangent on Christian theology. The reading isn't part of the documented [[English Curriculum 2022-2026]] (it doesn't appear on the [[Reading List]]), suggesting summer or independent reading; *We* is the foundational dystopia behind Orwell's *1984* (which ChatGPT explicitly compares S to O'Brien).

## What he was working out
Traveler pushes past a surface plot question into a genuine textual ambiguity. The sequence of questions tracks his reasoning:
- **Who is S?** — a faceless agent of the Bureau of Guardians (secret police), known only by a letter, embodying the One State's surveillance and depersonalization.
- **Was he a rebel?** — initially told no, a loyal enforcer.
- **Did D-503 know S was a Guardian?** — no, D treats him as a familiar acquaintance before the betrayal lands.
- **Why does D go to S at the end?** — D, emotionally broken between State-loyalty and his love for I-330, turns himself in seeking relief, leading to the Great Operation that strips his imagination.
- **The contradiction (page 196):** Traveler catches the model out. He quotes D's line *"he was one of them,"* forcing the reconciliation that S was secretly **Mephi** (a rebel) all along — and then presses the real puzzle: *why does a rebel S turn D in?* The synthesized answer: D had become a liability to the movement, so S makes the cold utilitarian choice to sacrifice the individual for the cause — mirroring the State's own logic.

This is characteristic of how Traveler reads: he's working from the actual page numbers of his copy, distrusts a tidy answer, and zeroes in on the moral symmetry where rebels and the regime both treat the individual as disposable. That tension between the [[Individual vs. Society]] and the collective, and the question of [[Conformity and Tradition]] versus authentic feeling, are exactly the themes his English work circles repeatedly — and they resonate with his own [[Political and Economic Views]] as a self-described individualist.

## The theological tangent
The chat ends abruptly with an unrelated question — *"do Christians believe that Jesus is God?"* — answered with a standard breakdown of Trinitarian doctrine (Father/Son/Holy Spirit) and the dissenting groups (Jehovah's Witnesses, Unitarians, Mormons, early Arians). As a religious skeptic who studied [[World Religions (10th)]], Traveler's curiosity here fits his recurring engagement with [[Faith vs. Knowledge]], though the question is fact-checking rather than belief.
