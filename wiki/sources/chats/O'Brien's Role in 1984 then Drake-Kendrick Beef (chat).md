## 1984 review

Traveler asks, in sequence, about **O'Brien's role**, a general **analysis**, **notable quotes**, and a **summary of the three parts** of the novel. The answers cover the standard ground:

- **O'Brien** as the Inner Party's psychological trap — he feigns Brotherhood sympathy, lures Winston, then tortures and "re-educates" him in the Ministry of Love, embodying *doublethink* and the Party's claim that "reality exists in the human mind, and nowhere else."
- Core themes: **totalitarianism and absolute power**, **thought control** via Newspeak and doublethink, the **destruction of truth/history** ("Who controls the past controls the future"), **loss of individuality** ("He loved Big Brother"), and **language and technology as tools of oppression**.
- The three-part structure: Part One (life under the Party, Winston's diary thoughtcrime, meeting Julia), Part Two (the affair above Charrington's shop, Goldstein's book, false hope), Part Three (arrest, Room 101, betrayal, total submission).

The Orwellian material connects naturally to themes already central to Traveler's coursework and worldview — [[Individual vs. Society]], [[Conformity and Tradition]], [[Identity and Its Collapse]], and [[Power, Hierarchy, and Justice]] — and to his interest in surveillance, control, and free will as a [[Political and Economic Views|determinist]]. *1984* itself is not on his documented IB [[Reading List]] or [[English Curriculum 2022-2026]], so this reads as supplementary or personal-interest review rather than a set text.
