# Qualitative Stock Screening — Moats and Market Share Across 20 Companies

A long, assembly-line equity-research session (GPT-4o, 2025-07-09) in which Traveler ran a near-identical qualitative template across roughly twenty companies, one after another. The recurring prompt — "[ticker] pros and cons non-financial but include market share and competitive moat data" (later expanded with "and other qualitative measures and should I buy or sell") — shows him building a repeatable framework for **competitive-moat analysis** rather than chasing any single name. This is hands-on training for his work in the [[Investment Club]] and his stated [[UVA and the Quant Question|finance ambitions]].

## What he was doing
He treats each company as a fill-in-the-blank case study, deliberately asking for the **non-financial** side — moat width, market share, culture, regulatory exposure, key-person risk — to complement the valuation/financial work seen in his other chats (e.g. [[Stock Valuation Metrics, Bonds, Options, and Bloomberg ROIC (chat)]], [[Insurance Equity Research - Arch Capital and Palomar (chat)]]). The consistency of the template is the point: he is internalizing the Morningstar-style **moat vocabulary** (wide / narrow / no moat) and the drivers behind each (switching costs, intangible brand, efficient scale, network effects, cost advantage).

## Companies covered
Insurance/specialty finance: **Halozyme (HALO)**, **Kinsale Capital (KNSL)**, **Axis Capital (AXS)**, **Bank of America (BAC)**. Royalty/energy/land: **Black Stone Minerals (BSM)**, **Viper Energy (VNOM)**, **EQT Corp**, **Texas Pacific Land (TPL)**. Industrials: **Caterpillar (CAT)**, **Deere (DE)**, **Otis Worldwide (OTIS)**. Consumer/brand: **Hershey**, **Nike**, **Ulta Beauty**, **Coca-Cola**, **Celsius (CELH)**, **Apple (AAPL)**, **Alibaba (BABA)**. Platform/tech: **Uber (UBER)**, **Johnson & Johnson (JNJ)**.

## Throughlines he was absorbing
- **Insurance mechanics.** He paused mid-screen to get the [[IB Economics (SL)|underwriting]] math explained — loss ratio, expense ratio, and combined ratio (< 100% = underwriting profit) — then asked specifically *how Kinsale sustains a sub-80% combined ratio* (centralized in-house underwriting, no delegated authority, niche E&S risks, ~20% expense ratio, conservative reserve development). This continues his deep interest in the E&S insurance niche.
- **Moat taxonomy.** The session repeatedly contrasts durable wide moats (Caterpillar, Deere, Otis, Coca-Cola, Apple, J&J) against scale-and-relationship "no-moat" royalty plays (BSM 3/10, Viper, EQT) and emerging/narrow moats (Celsius "no moat" per Morningstar, Uber narrow via local network effects). He notices when sources disagree (J&J: wide per Morningstar, "no moat" per AlphaSpread) — useful skepticism for a [[Political and Economic Views|determinist]] who likes to interrogate authority.
- **Decision framing.** From Uber onward he appends "should I buy or sell," and the model answers with investor-type matrices (long-term bullish / value / trader) rather than a flat call — modeling that the verdict depends on the holder's thesis and time horizon.
