# Banyan Chapters 28-30, Double Integrals, and Cold War Marxism

The continuation of Traveler's *In the Shadow of the Banyan* note-making, one week after the [[Banyan Chapters 25-27, Vietnam War Cram, and Mao vs Marx (chat)|chapters 25–27 session]]. He pastes summaries for **chapters 28–30** and again asks for three notes per chapter. These are the novel's darkest and final movements: the crater work camp and anti-Vietnamese propaganda, Mama's bartering of Radana's anklets and the last jewelry for rice, the embankment collapse that buries four children, Big Uncle's suicide, the "female rains" as collective weeping, Raami's traumatic muteness, Mouk's purges (Comrade Keng shot in the head), and finally the Vietnamese liberation, Papa's coded map to Thailand, and the closing affirmation that "love is our one eternity." The distilled notes lean on dehumanization, fear-not-loyalty as the engine of the regime, and **memory and storytelling as survival** — connecting to [[Memory and Trauma]], [[Art as Survival]], and [[Power, Hierarchy, and Justice]].

Mid-session he abruptly asks "double integral," and the model gives a clean primer — integration over a 2D region, geometric meaning as volume under a surface, computing iteratively one variable at a time, Fubini's theorem, and the polar-coordinate Jacobian. This is well beyond [[IB Math (SL)]] scope and fits Traveler's habit of reaching ahead into the multivariable calculus he expects to need for the [[UVA and the Quant Question|quant track]] at UVA.

He closes with three Cold War questions tying the literature back to [[IB History (HL)]]: how Marxism spread through the Third World (anti-colonial anger, Soviet/Chinese backing, peasant guerrilla adaptation), Cuba's role as an exporter of revolution (Castro, Che, Angola), and who Pol Pot was — explicitly linking the Cambodian genocide back to Raami's lived experience in the novel.

The chat captures Traveler's signature blend: emotionally heavy literary analysis, an out-of-syllabus math curiosity, and exam-oriented history, all in one thread. It reinforces how he routinely uses *Banyan* as the human face of the abstract Cold War ideology he studies.
