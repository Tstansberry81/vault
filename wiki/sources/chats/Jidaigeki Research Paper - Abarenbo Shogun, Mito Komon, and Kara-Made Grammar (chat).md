# Jidaigeki Research Paper - Abarenbo Shogun, Mito Komon, and Kara-Made Grammar

A working session for an [[IB Japanese (SL)]] research paper (an ~800-character writeup) on **jidaigeki** (時代劇, Japanese Edo-period TV dramas). Traveler is comparing two long-running series and needs both English-language source material and the Japanese grammar to write the paper himself. The chat splits cleanly into a research half and a translation/grammar half.

## Research half: hunting English sources
He starts frustrated that his original show, *Edo o Kiru*, has "obscure Japanese PDFs" but little English scholarship, and asks for jidaigeki with real English analysis. The model floats **Abarenbo Shogun** (1978–2008), **Ooku: The Inner Chambers**, and the **Ryomaden** taiga drama, but Traveler narrows hard: he wants sources *specifically* about Abarenbo Shogun, and explicitly **no Wikipedia**. The honest answer is that almost no English academic writing focuses exclusively on the show — he's pointed at Halloran's "Chrysanthemum and Sword Revisited," Isolde Standish's genre work on jidaigeki myth-making, and fallback summaries from TV Tropes / Apple TV / IMDb. This is a recurring pattern in his [[IB Japanese (SL)]] work: doing genuine source-quality vetting (rejecting Wikipedia, demanding show-specific citations) rather than accepting whatever is easiest.

Key factual content he extracted for the paper:
- **Abarenbo Shogun**: Tokugawa Yoshimune (8th shogun) goes undercover as low-rank samurai Tokuda Shinnosuke to expose corruption firsthand; ~800 episodes, aired 1978–2008 (~30 years).
- **Mito Komon**: Tokugawa Mitsukuni travels disguised, reveals his identity via a seal (印籠); aired 1969–2011 (~42 years).
- Genre conventions: justice vs. corruption, rigid class hierarchy, stock characters (ronin, magistrates, ninja, fire brigades), and a moral "wrongdoing → investigation → reveal → order restored" formula that romanticizes Tokugawa stability — essentially a "moral classroom on TV."

He asked for a paper outline (not the full essay): ~200 characters on jidaigeki generally, then ~100 characters each on Mito Komon and Abarenbo Shogun in one paragraph, plus a comparison sentence. This is consistent with his usual mode — he wants scaffolding and details, not a ghostwritten essay.

## Grammar half: writing it in Japanese
The back half is a focused SL-level vocab and grammar drill for actually composing the paper:
- **Disguise vocabulary**: 変装する (to disguise oneself) and 正体を隠す (to hide one's true identity) — rejecting the literal マスクをかぶる ("sounds like Halloween") for the undercover-shogun premise.
- **Duration with から〜まで**: 「１９７８年から２００８年まで約３０年間放送されました」, plus the alternative 始まり/終わり framing and when each is natural. The model flagged から〜まで as the cleaner, more formal choice for an SL paper.
- **Causal "people like it because..."** structures (〜ので / なぜなら〜から / という理由で支持する).
- Core nouns: 正義 (justice), 歴史 (history), 時代劇 (period drama), 文字数 (character count), and narration verbs 語る / 描く / 述べる.
- Hiragana readings and definitions for his key sentence 彼は日本で腐敗を暴きました ("He exposed corruption in Japan") — confirming かれ・にほん・ふはい・あばきました and the nuance of 暴く (to expose hidden wrongdoing) vs. 日本で vs. 日本の腐敗.
