# Econ IA on AI and Unemployment - RQ Pivot and Sourcing

A working session for Traveler's [[IB Economics (SL)]] Internal Assessment, where he tries to build a paper on AI's effect on U.S. unemployment and gets talked out of his original research question. Dated 2025-10-23 (GPT-5).

## What he was doing
The chat opens with `options` — invoking his **custom ChatGPT "modes" system** (the numbered persona menu: 0 Cynic Core, 1 Researcher, 2 Data Analyst, 3 Advice Giver, 4 Coder, 5 Read/Write Editor). This same mode framework recurs across his work and is documented in chats like [[Building a Custom ChatGPT Options Mode System and Mode 5 Essay Editor (chat)]] and [[Canvas API Access Audit and Custom ChatGPT Mode System (chat)]]. Here he drives the session mostly in Mode 2 (Data Analyst) to hunt sources.

He asks first for resources on "unemployment caused by AI in the U.S. economy," then sharpens the constraint: the IA needs sources about **what HAS happened, not what will happen** — "it can't be too forwards-looking." This is a precise read of IB IA requirements: examiners reward observed data and real-world application, not speculative futurism.

## Key content
- **Backward-looking sources surfaced:** Brookings ("The effects of AI on firms and workers," 2010–18, finding AI adoption had *not* caused widespread job loss), Pew (2022 — 19% of U.S. workers "most exposed" to AI), Federal Reserve Bank of Dallas ("very little evidence of AI taking away jobs on a large scale to date"), and Brynjolfsson et al.'s 2025 "Canaries in the Coal Mine?" using high-frequency payroll data.
- **His draft RQ:** *"To what extent has the increase in efficiency in the economy caused by artificial intelligence affected unemployment in the U.S.?"* — and he flags he "might have to pivot."
- **The verdict:** the RQ is too broad and not IA-viable. "AI efficiency" is not a measurable variable (no FRED series), causality is unprovable in his timeframe, and the data linking AI to unemployment "doesn't really exist yet."
- **Suggested pivots** toward measurable, narrow, evaluative questions: AI investment vs. employment in tech-intensive sectors; AI and wage polarization/inequality; regional automation exposure vs. manufacturing-state unemployment; or firm-level productivity vs. labor demand — each tied to concrete BLS/FRED/Pew datasets and standard diagrams (derived demand for labor, factor markets, technological change).
