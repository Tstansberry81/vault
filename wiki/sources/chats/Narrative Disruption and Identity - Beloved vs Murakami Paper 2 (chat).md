# Narrative Disruption and Identity - Beloved vs Murakami Paper 2

A long, exam-day prep session for an [[IB History (HL)|IB]] [[Paper 2 Study Guide|HL English Paper 2]] essay, pairing [[Beloved]] (Toni Morrison) with stories from [[The Elephant Vanishes]] (Haruki Murakami) under the prompt: *how have writers created narrative disruption and to what effect?* Traveler frames his own angle — connecting **chronology disruption** and the **"curses"** in the texts to **changes in identity**. The conversation is the most detailed record in the vault of how he builds a comparative literature argument under pressure, and it later spills into [[IB Math (SL)|Math]] and [[IB Physics (HL)|Physics]] review for the same exam stretch.

## The English argument he constructs

His spine: both authors use narrative disruption (non-linear chronology + surreal "curses") to show identity as unstable, fragmented, and shaped by forces beyond conscious control. The chat refines this from a vague idea into a defensible, examiner-friendly thesis. Two body paragraphs:

1. **Chronology → identity fragmentation.** [[Beloved]]'s out-of-order revelation of the infanticide mirrors how trauma destroys linear identity ("Nothing ever dies"); Murakami's "The Elephant Vanishes" refuses causal resolution ("it had vanished into thin air"), so identity loses coherence when reality itself becomes unreliable.
2. **"Curses" → identity transformation.** Beloved-the-character as trauma made flesh that consumes Sethe ("I am Beloved, and she is mine"); the unexplained hunger in *The Second Bakery Attack* ("the volcano was gone") as a *false* resolution — instability suppressed, not resolved.

The session shows Traveler's instinct for nuance and his characteristic risk of over-tidying. He notices the bakery "curse" seems to bring peace; ChatGPT pushes him to call it "temporary stabilization through absurd action" rather than genuine healing, anchored by the line "there's always yearning." He correctly traces "the past isn't dead, it's not even past" to Faulkner's *Requiem for a Nun* (not Beloved), and recovers "Nothing ever dies" as the Morrison parallel. He works through whether Sethe ever recovers (deliberately ambiguous), and his proposed reading — that the only escape is "fighting trauma head on" — gets corrected toward Morrison's actual claim: identity is partially reclaimed through **collective intervention**, not isolated confrontation. He then tests whether *both* authors endorse collective support and is warned off flattening Murakami into self-help: the wife joining the robbery is "mutual participation in instability," not restoration. He refines his own conclusion across several drafts, even interrogating diction ("was reconciliation the right word?" — swapped for "partial reclamation"). The tone connects directly to his work on [[Identity and Its Collapse]], [[Memory and Trauma]], and [[Fate and Free Will]].

Later he opens new angles: the "journey" motif in "Tuesday's Women" vs *The Wind-Up Bird Chronicle* (passive realization of emptiness vs active descent into the well), and **objects that carry identity** — Paul D's tobacco-tin heart, Sethe's "chokecherry tree" scars, the mechanical wind-up bird — Morrison's objects *contain* identity while Murakami's *destabilize* it.
