# Summarizing Wilson's League of Nations Speech for a Class Talk

One of [[Traveler Stansberry]]'s earliest archived ChatGPT exchanges (March 2024, sophomore year), and a small but clear coursework artifact: he pastes the full text of Woodrow Wilson's 1919 speech defending the **Treaty of Versailles and the League of Nations** and asks the model to "summarize this for a 3-5 minute speech." This is almost certainly a US/world-history assignment predating his [[IB History (HL)]] coursework — the same WWI-aftermath terrain he would later study in depth.

The primary source is Wilson stumping for Senate ratification: he frames himself as representative of all Americans rather than a party; argues the treaty "settles the land titles of Europe" on the American principle that "every land belongs to the people that live on it"; defends the harsh terms on Germany as just punishment for a "criminal act against mankind"; and walks through the League's machinery — the nine-month "cool off" arbitration delay, the automatic economic boycott of any covenant-breaker, and **Article X**'s guarantee of members' territorial integrity against external aggression. He closes by attacking Senate "reservations" ("an assent with a big but") and insisting the treaty is take-it-or-leave-it, with his eerily accurate warning that "within another generation there will be another world war" without collective prevention. The model returns a clean five-paragraph prose summary suitable for reading aloud.

What it reveals is the through-line of his academic profile: even at 15 he was working with dense primary documents and offloading the compression/synthesis step to AI — the same workflow he would later scale across [[IB History (HL)]], the [[Extended Essay (Economics)]], and his [[Homework Hatch (startup)|HW Buddy]] edtech project. Thematically the speech sits at the intersection of self-determination, [[Power, Hierarchy, and Justice]], and the [[Individual vs. Society]] question of collective security versus national sovereignty.
