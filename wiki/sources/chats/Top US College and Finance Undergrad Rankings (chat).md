# Top US College and Finance Undergrad Rankings (chat)

A grab-bag June 2025 ChatGPT session (gpt-4o) whose substantive spine is [[Traveler Stansberry]] surveying U.S. college rankings during his pre-application research, followed by two tangents (a [[IB History (HL)]]-adjacent civil-rights doctrine question and a pop-culture detour into *Anchorman* quotes).

## What he was doing
The bulk of the chat is Traveler pushing the model for ranked lists: first the **Top 50 U.S. national universities** (U.S. News 2025), then — the part that actually maps to his goals — the **Top 25 undergraduate finance programs**. He repeatedly pressed the model to "give me a list of all of them," frustrated when it returned partial or hedged lists, and corrected its misread of "undergard"/"undergard" → undergrad to force it off graduate/MBA rankings and onto undergraduate majors. This is the same outcomes-and-prestige-driven research seen in his [[College Search]] and [[Boston University Finance Program and Top Finance Schools (chat)]] sessions, and it sits squarely in his finance-track ambitions documented in [[UVA and the Quant Question]].

## The finance ranking content
The undergrad finance list put **Wharton (Penn)** at #1, with **NYU Stern**, **Michigan Ross**, **UT Austin McCombs**, **Cornell**, **Berkeley Haas**, **Georgetown McDonough**, **Carnegie Mellon Tepper**, and then — notably — **UVA McIntire** at roughly #9. McIntire's appearance is the personally load-bearing detail: it confirms his eventual destination ([[UVA and the Quant Question]]) is consistently ranked a top-10 undergraduate finance program, validating the [[St. Paul's School]] senior's college choice on the exact metric (recruiter pipelines, Wall Street placement) he cares about. The model also flagged Carnegie Mellon and Berkeley for **quant/fintech prep**, the niche tied to his "quant question."

## The civil rights tangent
Mid-chat he pivoted to **Title VII of the Civil Rights Act of 1964** and the **disparate impact** doctrine — neutral policies that disproportionately burden a protected class without discriminatory intent (*Griggs v. Duke Power*, 1971) — then asked specifically for the **negatives** of disparate impact (merit vs. outcomes, statistical over-reach, *Ricci v. DeStefano* reverse-discrimination risk, chilling effects). The deliberate ask for criticisms of an anti-discrimination doctrine fits his [[Political and Economic Views]] as an [[Individual vs. Society|individualist]] skeptical of outcome-based regulation, echoing the supply-side and merit-leaning instincts elsewhere in his econ work ([[IB Economics (SL)]]).

## Closing tangent
The session ends with a request for "best *Anchorman* quotes" — pure entertainment, no academic content.

This chat is mostly reference-list noise, but it usefully timestamps (June 2025, summer before senior year) Traveler's active finance-school ranking research and his repeated insistence on full, undergrad-specific lists.
