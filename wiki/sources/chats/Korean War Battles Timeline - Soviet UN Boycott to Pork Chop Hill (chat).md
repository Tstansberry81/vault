# Korean War Battles Timeline - Soviet UN Boycott to Pork Chop Hill

A [[IB History (HL)]] research chat in which Traveler mines the Korean War for a presentation/essay — exactly the kind of content-gathering he does ahead of [[St. Paul's School]] history assessments. The thread moves from a single causal puzzle to a full battle timeline, and the model frames every answer with an "IB-safe conclusion sentence," which Traveler clearly solicits as ready-to-use thesis lines.

## The Soviet self-own
The opening question asks why the USSR's 1950 boycott of the UN Security Council — a protest against the UN seating Nationalist China (Taiwan) instead of the new communist PRC — was a "huge mistake." Answer: by walking out, the Soviets forfeited their veto at the exact moment North Korea invaded South Korea (June 1950), letting the Council declare North Korea the aggressor and authorize a US-led but **UN-sanctioned** intervention. The takeaway line: the boycott "transformed a regional civil war into an international conflict." A clean case of ideology overriding strategy, useful for a Cold War causation argument.

## The battle timeline
Traveler then requests a chronological breakdown of seven engagements with location, conditions, and outcomes:
- **Incheon (Sep 1950)** — amphibious landing, the one *non*-mountainous battle; decisive UN victory that saved South Korea and triggered the push north.
- **Chosin Reservoir (Nov–Dec 1950)** — −30°F fighting retreat; China enters the war (this overlaps his separate [[Battle of Chosin Reservoir Presentation Research (chat)]]).
- **Imjin River (Apr 1951)** — Commonwealth units blunt the Chinese Spring Offensive, saving Seoul.
- **Bloody Ridge / Heartbreak Ridge (Aug–Oct 1951)** — opening of attritional trench warfare; hills "rented by the hour."
- **Old Baldy / Pork Chop Hill (1952–1953)** — symbolic hilltop fights near the 38th Parallel, men dying for terrain irrelevant to the armistice talks.

The synthesis arc: the war shifted from "decisive maneuver warfare" (Incheon) to "prolonged attritional combat" (Pork Chop Hill), where "symbolic terrain replaced strategic objectives." He also confirms that most battles were fought on mountainous/ridge terrain (~70% of the peninsula is mountainous), and that elevation became "a substitute for strategy."

## Leadership and sourcing
Final exchanges clarify the South Korean force as the **Republic of Korea Army (ROKA)**, distinguishing nominal commander-in-chief **Syngman Rhee** from actual operational control under the UN Command (MacArthur, then Ridgway), with **Paik Sun-yup** as the real ROK field commander to name. Notably, when Traveler demanded a battle map and insisted "dont make it up find one and make sure its accurate," the model pointed to the Korean War Legacy Foundation and the US Army Center of Military History rather than inventing one — Traveler enforcing source reliability, consistent with his skeptical, evidence-first instincts seen in [[Core Convictions]].
