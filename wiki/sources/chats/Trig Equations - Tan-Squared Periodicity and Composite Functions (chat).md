# Trig Equations - Tan-Squared Periodicity and Composite Functions

A focused [[IB Math (SL)]] homework session in which Traveler works through a set of trigonometric equations on the domain $0 \le x \le 2\pi$. The conversation is notable less for the answers than for the genuinely sharp conceptual pushback he gives — this is a window into how he learns math, refusing to accept a result he doesn't understand.

## What he was solving
- **$\tan^2(2x) = 1$** on $[0, 2\pi)$ — the anchor problem, yielding the eight evenly-spaced solutions $x = \tfrac{\pi}{8} + n\tfrac{\pi}{4}$ (22.5°, 67.5°, ... 337.5°).
- **$\sin^2 x - \sin x - 2 = 0$** — solved by the substitution $y = \sin x$, factoring $(y-2)(y+1)=0$, discarding $y=2$ as out of range, leaving the single solution $x = \tfrac{3\pi}{2}$.
- **$4\sin^2 x = 1$** → $\sin x = \pm\tfrac12$, four solutions ($\tfrac{\pi}{6}, \tfrac{5\pi}{6}, \tfrac{7\pi}{6}, \tfrac{11\pi}{6}$).
- A composite-function image problem: with $f(x)=\cos x$, $g(x)=2x$, solving $(f\circ g)(x)=\cos(2x)=1$ and $(g\circ f)(x)=2\cos x=1$.

## What it reveals
The core of the chat is Traveler interrogating *why* the tangent-squared equation has period $\pi/2$ rather than $\pi$. He correctly objects that "shouldn't it just be $\pi$ because that's the period of tangent?", then identifies the exact place his own work stalled — he had the right base angles ($\tfrac{\pi}{4}, \tfrac{3\pi}{4}, \tfrac{5\pi}{4}, \tfrac{7\pi}{4}$) but assumed the period of $x$ was over 4, missing half the solution set. He keeps probing: would treating the $+1$ and $-1$ cases independently give the same answers (yes), and *why* does squaring "split it in two." The takeaway — squaring a trig function folds its graph over the x-axis, halving the period because the sign no longer matters — is exactly the kind of structural insight he chases rather than memorizing.
