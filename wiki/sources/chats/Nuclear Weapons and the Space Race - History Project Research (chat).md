# Nuclear Weapons and the Space Race - History Project Research

A research-gathering session for a [[IB History (HL)]] project on **nuclear bomb development as it relates to the Space Race**. Traveler opens with a broad ask ("what do I need to know") and works the model down to specifics, ending on the technical question of whether the moon landing used nuclear technology. The chat is a window into how he scaffolds a history assignment — soliciting a big-picture frame, a timeline, thesis options, and a teacher-friendly poster structure before drilling into particulars.

## Core argument the chat assembles
The throughline is **dual-use engineering**: the same rocketry that delivered nuclear warheads also launched satellites and humans. ICBMs were "space rockets with worse motives." Key linkage points: the Soviet **R-7** that launched Sputnik (1957) was a missile design; reconnaissance satellites tracked nuclear tests; missile launches doubled as political signaling. The framing positions **Sputnik** less as a scientific milestone than a political shock that reshaped US budgets and nuclear posture.

## Timeline beats gathered
- **1945** — Trinity test, then Hiroshima and Nagasaki ([[Robert Oppenheimer]]-era Manhattan Project: Oppenheimer, Fermi, Teller).
- **1957** — Sputnik; the psychological point-of-no-return driving missile/NASA funding.
- **1957–1964** — ICBM deployment (R-7, Atlas, Titan) and warhead miniaturization.
- **1963** — Partial Test Ban Treaty.
- Post-1963 — the model walks forward through MIRVs, **Mutually Assured Destruction (MAD)**, the NPT (1968), SALT I/II, the ABM Treaty (1972), Reagan's SDI ("Star Wars," 1983), the Soviet collapse and START treaties, and modern hypersonic/space-militarization echoes. The model notes that for a project scoped to "nuclear development pertaining to the Space Race," the real story runs **1945–1972**.

## The technical payoff
Traveler narrows to the 1969 moon landing and the **Saturn V**: a three-stage chemical rocket (S-IC with 5× F-1 kerosene/LOX engines; S-II and S-IVB on liquid hydrogen/LOX), guided by an IBM-built Instrument Unit, designed under Wernher von Braun at Marshall Space Flight Center with lineage from the V-2 and Atlas/Titan programs. His pointed final question — "so did it use nuclear technology" — gets a clean no: Saturn V was **chemical propulsion**, only "nuclear-funded," not nuclear-powered. The model flags **NERVA** (nuclear thermal rocket studies that never flew) and RTGs on later deep-space probes (Voyager) as the places nuclear tech actually appeared.

## What it shows
