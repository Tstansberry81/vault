# Elon Musk Billionaire Ethics Counterargument - IB English Essay

A long evidence-gathering and drafting session for a short argumentative essay in **[[St. Paul's School]]** IB SL English (Mr. Bryant's class), written **against** a classmate's (Aamir's) essay titled "Why Billionaires Are Not Ethical." Aamir's piece argued that Elon Musk "hoarding" wealth is fundamentally unethical because he could "solve world hunger" by donating less than half his net worth. Traveler's rebuttal, titled "Why Billionaires Are Not Unethical," defends a **libertarian-individualist** ethical position — that no one gets to dictate how Musk spends his own money, and that ethical obligation cannot be coerced onto individuals for the sake of others.

This chat is a clean window into Traveler's [[Political and Economic Views]] and [[Core Convictions]]: he is a [[determinist]] individualist who instinctively reaches for autonomy, property rights, and Milton Friedman over redistribution. Notably, when ChatGPT kept trying to *write the argument for him*, he pushed back — "i dont want you to make the argument for me, i want you to provide evidence for me so i can back my already existing claims." The thesis, structure, and convictions are his; he used the model as a research assistant and line editor.

## The argument he built
- **Thesis (his own wording):** "Billionaires are not inherently unethical because ethics are subjective, and ethical views cannot be imposed on individuals for choosing not to provide for others."
- **World hunger rebuttal:** Per the UN/FAO, ending hunger costs ~$40 billion *per year* through 2030 — not a one-time check. Musk could fund a year only by selling ~$160 billion of Tesla stock, and money alone doesn't fix supply shortages, war, corruption, and logistics.
- **Contribution-by-creation:** Musk employs 140,000+ at Tesla (US), co-founded PayPal (436M active accounts, Q1 2025), and built SpaceX (first to catch/reuse rocket boosters — "one of the greatest human achievements of the century"). Wealth that is *productive*, not parasitic.
- **Liquidity point:** Only ~$5.2B of Musk's ~$470-500B net worth is liquid (≈1.1%), proportionally lower than Bezos, Buffett, Gates. Dumping Tesla stock to donate would crater the company (cf. the ~30% drop after his 2021 ~$22B sale), harming employees and pension funds.
- **Friedman frame:** "Nobody spends somebody else's money as carefully as he spends his own" (*Free to Choose*, 1980) — the four-ways-to-spend-money argument against forced redistribution.

He also asked ChatGPT to walk through ethical frameworks (deontology, utilitarianism, virtue ethics, relativism) to ground the "ethics are subjective" claim — connecting to [[Theory of Knowledge]] and [[Faith vs. Knowledge]]-adjacent moral epistemology.

> [!note] Sourcing honesty
> Mid-chat he caught a fabricated citation: the "$40 billion/year to achieve Zero Hunger" line could not be verified verbatim in the FAO SOFI 2021 report. ChatGPT admitted the quote was likely a paraphrase from a secondary source (Health Policy Watch), and advised hedging ("estimated at"). A small but real instance of Traveler doing citation due diligence rather than trusting the model.
