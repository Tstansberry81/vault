# FTA vs Customs Union and Chinese Currency Manipulation (chat)

A short, exam-focused [[IB Economics (SL)]] study session on the international-trade unit, dated Feb 2026. Traveler drills the distinctions between levels of economic integration and the mechanics of currency manipulation — the kind of definitional cleanup that shows up in Paper 1 short-answer and evaluation questions. The conversation pairs naturally with [[PTAs and FTAs Explained for IB Econ (chat)|PTAs and FTAs Explained for IB Econ]] and [[Free Trade Debate Prep - Tariffs, Subsidies, Reagan, and Trump Inflation (chat)|Free Trade Debate Prep - Tariffs, Subsidies, Reagan, and Trump Inflation]].

## What he asked

- **FTA vs customs union** — examples and main differences. The model gives the textbook contrast: a **Free Trade Area** removes internal tariffs but lets each member keep its own external trade policy (example: USMCA, which replaced NAFTA in 2020; also Australia–New Zealand CER, 1983). A **customs union** adds a **Common External Tariff (CET)** so all members tax non-members identically (example: the EU customs union; Mercosur in South America). Key downstream point Traveler is told students forget: FTAs need **rules of origin** to stop "tariff hopping" through the lowest-tariff member, while customs unions don't because the external tariff is uniform.
- **Why China is accused of currency manipulation** — the answer walks through the renminbi/yuan being managed (not freely floating) by the People's Bank of China, the accusation that China kept the yuan **undervalued** to cheapen exports and improve its current account, the historical mechanism (buying USD, building reserves, especially in the 2000s), the US complaint (unfair advantage, trade deficit, manufacturing harm; the 2019 Treasury "currency manipulator" label later reversed), and the nuance that today China often intervenes to *strengthen* the yuan against capital flight — so the "permanent undervaluation machine" narrative is outdated.
- **Is an economic union a trade bloc?** — yes, the most integrated form. The model lays out the full **ladder of integration**: FTA → Customs Union → Common Market (adds free movement of labor and capital) → Economic Union (adds coordinated economic policy, often shared monetary policy, e.g. the EU and the euro).
