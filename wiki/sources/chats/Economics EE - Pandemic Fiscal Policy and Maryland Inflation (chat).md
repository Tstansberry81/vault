# Economics EE - Pandemic Fiscal Policy and Maryland Inflation

A working session (GPT-4o, 29 Aug 2025) on Traveler's [[Extended Essay (Economics)]], whose research question is: *"To what extent did the Coronavirus pandemic's reactionary fiscal policy such as the CARES Act and the American Rescue Plan Act affect inflation in Maryland during 2020-2023?"* This chat is the single richest window into the EE's actual draft text, argument structure, and sourcing struggles. It overlaps heavily with the localized study captured in [[COVID Stimulus and Inflation in Maryland - Paper Research (chat)]].

## The essay's thesis and structure

Traveler pastes nearly the full draft. The thesis: pandemic reactionary fiscal policy "caused significant inflation in 2022 and minor inflation in 2021 and 2023 due to the excessive increase in aggregate demand, increasing the national debt, raising energy prices, and tightening the labor market, which affected price inflation in Maryland." Four arguments, each with counter-argument and rebuttal:

1. **Demand-pull inflation** — CARES (~$270B) + ARPA (~$400B / ~$4T total demand into a ~$20T economy) overheated an economy with supply, not demand, problems. Counter: cost-push from supply chains mattered more; rebuttal cites Goodwin et al. *Macroeconomics in Context* that spending added ~20% to the economy.
2. **Debt-driven inflation** — national debt up >$4T in 2019-2020; "debt is inherently inflationary."
3. **Energy prices** — gas to $5/gal in MD June 2022, tracking the 6% YoY Baltimore-Columbia-Towson CPI peak; leans on MIT Sloan (Kritzman/Vereckey) that 42% of inflation traced to federal spending, not the supply chain.
4. **Labor-market tightness** — stimulus discouraged some work, creating wage-price-spiral risk, while honestly conceding the effect "wasn't massive."

This shows Traveler's argumentative style: strong [[IB Economics (SL)]] vocabulary (demand-pull vs cost-push, wage-price spiral, Keynesian intervention), explicit counter/rebuttal scaffolding, and a moralized conclusion ("history repeats itself"). It connects to his broader [[Political and Economic Views]] — a skeptic of fiscal overreach who blames stimulus over supply shocks.

## What he was actually asking for

Most of the chat is him hunting for **Maryland-specific sources** to localize Arguments 2 and 4, insisting repeatedly "needs to be maryland specific" and "high accuracy and credible sources." The assistant returns a mix of real reports (Maryland Comptroller *State of the Economy*, the JEC Maryland Inflation Update, BLS Baltimore CPI) and several plausible-but-shaky citations. Notably, when he demands verbatim quotes, the model admits it cannot find them and offers paraphrases — a moment where Traveler is pushing back on AI source reliability.

> [!warning] Reliability caution
> The assistant supplied figures (e.g., "$7.7 billion" / "$36 billion" in MD federal relief, MD GDP growth of 1.6% vs 13.9% national) and "quotes" it later conceded were not verifiable verbatim, and cited some links as "currently offline." Any of these that made it into the final EE should be independently checked.

## Economics tutoring thread

The back half is genuine concept-building for the essay's diagrams: the money market (vertical inelastic MS, downward MD), how raising rates contracts the money supply, the Quantity Theory of Money (MV = PQ), why MS ≠ AD (AD = C + I + G + (X−M)), endogenous money / "loans create deposits," and a careful walk-through of how the Fed lowers the EFFR via open-market operations. He confirms Jerome Powell chaired the Fed throughout. The final exchange is strategic: for an [[IB Economics (SL)]] EE, should graphs be theoretical or quantitatively accurate? Answer — both: theoretical model diagrams (AD-AS, MS-MD) plus real-data charts (BLS CPI, GasBuddy, FRED) with stimulus-event overlays, which strengthens the EE assessment criteria.

## What it reveals

Traveler is methodical and intellectually honest in the draft (he flags where an argument is weak), but reliant on the chatbot for both source-finding and remedial macro theory mid-EE. The session captures the EE in mid-draft, after [[COVID Stimulus and Inflation in Maryland - Paper Research (chat)]], showing how he iterated from a national story toward state-level evidence. It also foreshadows his finance trajectory toward [[UVA and the Quant Question]] — the comfort with monetary-policy mechanics and data graphing is the same toolkit he later applies to investing.
