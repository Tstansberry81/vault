# Narrow Road Free Will Essay — Identity, Darky, and Nakamura

A coaching session for Traveler's **IB HL English 2** essay (for Mr. Mezeske, due 3 December 2025) on [[The Narrow Road to the Deep North]] by Richard Flanagan. The driving prompt: *"In what ways does Flanagan explore the identities of characters to illustrate the conflict of fate and free will?"* This chat shows the full arc from grammar-checking a single sentence to pasting a complete draft for feedback to processing the teacher's revision notes.

## The thesis
Traveler's argument is unusually conceptual for HL: **the perception of free will is more advantageous than the acceptance of fate**, and Flanagan uses character *identity* as the mechanism through which that conflict plays out. This is a near-direct echo of the novel's own bleak philosophy (free will is probably an illusion, but believing in it is what keeps humans alive in extremity) and connects tightly to Traveler's documented [[Fate and Free Will]] preoccupation and his self-identification as a determinist (see [[Political and Economic Views]], [[Core Convictions]]). The essay also leans on [[Identity and Its Collapse]] as its structural spine.

## The character triad → dyad
The original draft used three figures as a "comparative identity lab":
- **Dorrigo Evans** — fractured identity; survives by clinging to chosen illusions of agency (the promise to marry Amy at p.148; the self-fashioned "Big Fella" leader persona at pp.48–49). Traveler's sharper move: the Big Fella is an act of free will *because Dorrigo chooses to inhabit the identity Amy already imagines for him* — agency is the response to a projected role, not invention from nothing.
- **Darky Gardiner** — stable identity built on micro-choices and refusal to surrender ("never give up on the small things," p.171). Dies anyway (drowns in the benjo, p.264), but the benefit Traveler argues is *psychological, not literal* survival — resilience, dignity, longer endurance than collapsed men like Tiny Middleton.
- **Nakamura** — fatalistic identity fused to the "Japanese spirit" (p.103), which excuses cruelty; collapses morally and mentally when that fate-narrative dissolves (pp.319, 348, 350 — final ravings, beating his wife and daughters).

Mr. Mezeske's verdict, relayed mid-chat: **cut Darky entirely, fully flesh out Dorrigo and Nakamura**, and stop leaning on plot summary. The chat endorsed this as a tightening into a clean dialectic — agency vs. fate, chosen vs. inherited identity, moral responsibility vs. moral surrender.

## What it reveals about Traveler's process
- He explicitly does not want prose rewritten ("i can refine my writing i just need to know if my argument is solid") — he uses the model as an argument-validator and structural editor, not a ghostwriter. Consistent with his other essay-coaching chats (cf. [[Death of a Salesman Essay Coaching - Iterative Paragraph Revision (chat)|Death of a Salesman Essay Coaching - Iterative Paragraph Revision]]).
- He asked for a "nicer way to say 'shit himself'" for the Darky benjo scene — a concrete register-management instinct.
- The closing exchange is the most revealing: he admits he *hasn't actually answered the "in what ways" prompt* and is summarizing instead of analyzing. The reply gives him the analysis-first template (claim answering prompt → quote → analysis of how identity reveals fate/free will → thesis-tie) — the 6-to-7 jump under [[Individual Oral (IO)]]-style IB criteria B and C.
- Self-assessed draft grade: 6/7, with 7/7 potential after fixes. Typos flagged: "Evansm," "typicaly."

This sits alongside [[Antiderivatives, the Long March, and a Narrow Road Circle-Line Essay (chat)]] as part of Traveler's sustained work on the Flanagan novel, and reflects his broader habit of reading literature through a free-will/determinism lens.
