# UT Austin Internal Transfer into McCombs Business (chat)

A summer 2025 ChatGPT exchange (gpt-5) in which [[Traveler Stansberry]] probes the mechanics of **internal transfer at UT Austin** — whether you can switch colleges after admission, and specifically how to get from a less-selective program into the **McCombs School of Business**. The chat fits squarely into his [[College Search]] and finance-undergrad fixation (see [[Top US College and Finance Undergrad Rankings (chat)]], [[Boston University Finance Program and Top Finance Schools (chat)]]): even with [[UVA and the Quant Question|UVA]] as his stated destination, he is canvassing backdoor routes into elite business programs.

## What he was probing
- **Can you switch schools at UT Austin?** Yes, via an *internal transfer*, but it is gated: apply within your first four long semesters, hold ≤60 in-residence hours, and at most two attempts per target college. ≤45 hours lets you apply regardless of GPA; >45 hours needs a 2.0+ UT GPA.
- **Hypothetical: admitted to an agricultural program, want McCombs.** This is the revealing move — he is reasoning about applying to an *easier-to-get-into* college as a foot in the door, then transferring up. ChatGPT flags McCombs as one of the most competitive internal transfers on campus: ~10–20% acceptance, with successful applicants often at 3.8–4.0 GPA, requiring calculus (M 408K/C), micro/macro econ (ECO 304K/L), financial accounting (ACC 311), plus essays and a resume.
- **Strategic takeaway** he was handed: from day one take the McCombs prerequisites, protect GPA in math/econ, join business orgs early — i.e. treat the first year as a transfer audition.

This is consistent with his broader pattern of optimizing the path into quantitative-finance programs and reflects his pragmatic, system-gaming approach to admissions.

## Tangents in the same chat
- **Does IQ correlate to addiction?** He asked this offhand. The answer: higher adolescent IQ predicts more *experimentation* (cannabis, stimulants — White et al. 2011), but *addiction/dependence* tends to correlate *negatively* with IQ (lower childhood IQ → higher persistent dependence, Moffitt et al. 2011).
