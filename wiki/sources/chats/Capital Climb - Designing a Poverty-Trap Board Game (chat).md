# Capital Climb - Designing a Poverty-Trap Board Game

A production-heavy [[coursework-econ|economics]] design session in which [[Traveler Stansberry]] built **Capital Climb**, an original Monopoly-style board game modeling the poverty trap — almost certainly an [[IB Economics (SL)]] project. He fed the model the full ruleset for a thorough summary, then iterated on deck consolidation, card specifications, printable layouts, and a board design, having the model generate Word/PDF files for hand-cutting and printing the same day.

The game's design is a genuinely sophisticated piece of applied microeconomics. Four players begin at Age 0 but roll for a starting social class (Upper, Middle, Lower-Middle, Lower), each seeded with different amounts of three currencies — **Money, Education, and Social Credit** — so the game is "unfair by design." The win condition is Total Life Value (TLV / Retirement): three properties, a college degree, and a family with two kids, achieved at the *youngest* age (each board loop ages you). The themes he and the model articulated: the poverty trap, structural inequality, equal rules ≠ equal opportunity, multidimensional capital, compounding advantage (investments paying out per rotation), chance vs choice (a 1-in-50 lottery), time as inequality, and institutional barriers (two jail spaces; $10,000 bail favors the wealthy). This is a direct, gamified expression of his [[Political and Economic Views]] and the [[Individual vs. Society]] / [[Power, Hierarchy, and Justice]] themes.

The practical thread is equally telling. He wrestled the deck count down from six to five to (proposed) three to ease the hand-cutting burden, but ultimately insisted on keeping Money, Education, and Social Credit as separate decks specifically to preserve **player agency** — letting players choose which capital to pursue, modeling resource prioritization under constraint. He then dictated precise card specs: 100 $1,000 money cards plus larger-denomination decks; 100 cards each for Social Credit and Education; 100 opportunity cards split across Jobs (McDonald's Worker → CEO, scaling income and education requirements), College tiers (Community College → Ivy), Property, Family (Partner → Child 3), and Stocks (Apple, Nvidia, Tesla, Amazon, Lockheed Martin — overlapping with his [[Investment Club]] / finance interests); and a 50-card lottery deck with one all-gold $1M winner. The model flagged a real spec inconsistency (4 named college tiers but 20 cards at 4-per-tier requires 5 tiers) and produced a "production version" with cut guides and separator pages, plus a printable 20×20 board split into four 5×5-inch quadrants.
