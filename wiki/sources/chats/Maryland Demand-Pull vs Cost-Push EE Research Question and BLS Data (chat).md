# Maryland Demand-Pull vs Cost-Push EE Research Question and BLS Data

A working session for the [[Extended Essay (Economics)]] in which Traveler hammers out both the **research question** and the **causal mechanics** of pandemic inflation in Maryland. The chat title ("BLS counties in region") is misleading — it opens with a lookup of which counties make up the **Baltimore-Columbia-Towson** BLS region (Anne Arundel, Baltimore, Carroll, Harford, Howard, Queen Anne's, plus Baltimore City) because that metro is the only proxy for a "Maryland" CPI, and the EE leans on it throughout. This connects directly to his [[IB Economics (SL)]] coursework and the broader [[UVA and the Quant Question|finance trajectory]].

## What he was doing

He fed ChatGPT draft sentences and asked it to fact-check, sharpen, and convert them into examiner-friendly macro logic — a recurring pattern in his EE chats (see also [[Economics EE Four Arguments and BLS-FRED Data Sourcing (chat)|Economics EE Four Arguments and BLS-FRED Data Sourcing]], [[Economics EE - Pandemic Fiscal Policy and Maryland Inflation (chat)|Economics EE - Pandemic Fiscal Policy and Maryland Inflation]]). Key threads:

- **Two transmission channels.** He works through how **debt raises costs of production** via interest expense (cost-push, AS shifts left) versus how **debt-fueled spending raises aggregate demand** faster than supply (demand-pull, AD shifts right). ChatGPT flags a logical error in one of his draft sentences: rate hikes don't make *input costs* (steel, wages) rise — they raise *financing costs* and are meant to *suppress* demand, not fuel cost-push inflation.
- **Energy as the pivot.** He confirms the **41.6% YoY energy inflation peak in June 2022** (largest since April 1980) and the **9.1% all-items national peak**, then localizes it: **Baltimore-Columbia-Towson CPI-U rose 10.6% YoY in June 2022**. He gets an MLA citation for the BLS June 2022 CPI release and its in-text form (U.S. Department of Labor).
- **"Demand-pull in disguise."** A nuance he was clearly trying to use as an essay argument: energy is an inelastic, universal input, so an energy shock starts as cost-push but *behaves* like demand-pull because consumers keep paying inflated prices rather than cutting consumption.
- **Labor-market tightness.** He covers the **unemployed-to-job-openings ratio** (~0.6–0.7 in 2022–23 vs ~1.2 pre-pandemic), what a sub-1 ratio means, and the **wage-price spiral** — overlapping with [[Unemployment Concepts for Econ SL - Discouraged Workers, Beveridge Curve, LFPR (chat)|Unemployment Concepts for Econ SL - Discouraged Workers, Beveridge Curve, LFPR]] and [[EE Submission Tips - Maryland Labor Market Tightness and Federal Debt (chat)|EE Submission Tips - Maryland Labor Market Tightness and Federal Debt]].

## Research-question evolution

The core payoff of the chat is iterating the EE question from vague to examiner-safe:

1. *"To what extent did demand-pull inflation during the pandemic affect Maryland during 2020-2023?"* — flagged as too wide; "extent" undefined, demand-pull hard to isolate from cost-push.
2. He asks whether **adding cost-push** would broaden it — ChatGPT argues the opposite: the demand-pull-vs-cost-push **dichotomy makes the essay evaluative rather than descriptive**.
3. Landing version: *"To what extent did demand-pull and cost-push factors contribute to inflation in Maryland (as measured by the Baltimore-Columbia-Towson CPI-U) during 2020–2023?"* — with the data source embedded in the question.

He also presses on whether the **2020–2025 timeframe is too long**, asking specifically about any IB-imposed limit in the Economics EE subject section. ChatGPT finds no formal rule, only the "5-year rule" student hearsay, and advises narrowing to 2020–2023 for analytical depth.

## What it reveals

This is Traveler using the model as a **Socratic examiner and grammar checker** — he repeatedly asks "is this true?", "is this specific enough?", and even "is 'tight' proper grammar?" rather than asking it to write for him. It shows him genuinely wrestling with the cost-push/demand-pull distinction and the data-availability constraint (no statewide Maryland CPI) that shaped his entire EE design, consistent with his analytical, [[Intellectual Profile|first-principles]] approach.
