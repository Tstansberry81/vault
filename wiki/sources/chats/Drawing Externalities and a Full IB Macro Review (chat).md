# Drawing Externalities and a Full IB Macro Review

A [[IB Economics (SL)]] exam-prep session in which Traveler works through how to draw and reason about **externality diagrams**, then asks for an "extensive review" of every macro topic in his class study guide. The chat is a diagnostic of where his understanding was shaky just before exams.

## Externality diagrams

The conversation opens with "how to draw externalities" and walks through the standard IB toolkit: a **negative externality of production** (MSC above MPC, MSB = MPB, overproduction, deadweight-loss triangle from Q_social to Q_market) and a **positive externality of consumption** (MSB above MPB, MSC = MPC, underconsumption). Government fixes are mapped to type — **Pigouvian tax**, regulation, and cap-and-trade for negative; subsidy, public provision, and mandates for positive.

Two of Traveler's own statements get corrected, which reveals his working misconceptions:
- He proposed that **"if MPB is greater than MSB ... it's a negative externality."** This is wrong — MSB = MPB + external benefit, so MSB is never below MPB in the syllabus. The clean rule: **MSC > MPC → negative; MSB > MPB → positive.**
- He described drawing diagrams by **"connecting the new equilibrium to whatever curve shifted."** Corrected to: you never *connect* equilibria, you *re-intersect* — the social optimum is the intersection of the shifted social curve with the unchanged private curve.

These are the exact stumbling points the ChatGPT tutor flagged, and they show Traveler reasoning his way toward the right model rather than just memorizing it.

## The full macro survival guide

The back half is a topic-by-topic review keyed to his uploaded study guide, organized around the **three macro concerns** (growth/GDP, unemployment, inflation):
- **Unemployment** — labor force definition, discouraged workers (and how they distort the unemployment rate downward), the labor-market wage graph, and the frictional/structural/cyclical typology.
- **Inflation** — sustained price-level rise; menu/shoe-leather costs, redistribution from savers to borrowers; CPI measurement and its biases; demand-pull (AD right) vs cost-push (AS left).
- **Economic growth** — nominal vs real GDP, AS/AD with vertical LRAS, inflationary (AD-driven, temporary) vs non-inflationary (LRAS-driven) growth.
- **Inequality** (equality vs equity), **international trade** (comparative advantage, gains from trade), and **protectionism** (arguments for/against, plus tariff, subsidy, quota, and administrative-barrier diagrams with revenue/DWL shading).
