## Thoreau and the politics of generosity
The session begins with a [[Henry David Thoreau]] line: *"He who gives himself entirely to his fellow-men appears to them useless and selfish; but he who gives himself partially to them is pronounced a benefactor and philanthropist."* Traveler asks for the meaning, then drills into when and why [[Civil Disobedience]] was written (1849, out of Thoreau's 1846 jailing for refusing the poll tax that funded the Mexican–American War and slavery). The framing — that society rewards *manageable* goodness and punishes total moral commitment, that conscience outranks the state — sits squarely in his recurring interests around [[Individual vs. Society]] and [[Power, Hierarchy, and Justice]], and echoes his engagement with [[Self-Reliance]] and Emersonian/Transcendentalist [[Intellectual Interests]].

He follows with a [[Confucius]] quote (poverty is shameful in a just state; wealth is shameful in an unjust one), read as a "moral audit" of how success is never morally neutral — a theme that resonates with his [[Political and Economic Views]].

## IB Math: indefinite integrals
The bulk of the chat is a [[IB Math (SL)]] integration set. Traveler first asks *why constants appear in indefinite integrals* (differentiation destroys constants; +C remembers the lost information). He then works a full textbook problem set (a–l) on integrals solved by expansion, rewriting as powers, and the **reverse chain rule / u-substitution** — including $\int(2x+1)^2$, $\int(x+1/x)^2$, and quotients over $x^{3/2}$. A scanned worksheet image was attached. Additional one-offs cover the derivative of $3\ln x$, $\int \frac{2}{3x+8}$, $\int (4x+3)^{-2}$, $\int (2x+1)^{1/3}$, $\int e^{5x}+\frac{8}{5x-3}$, and $\int(4x+3)^{-1/3}$ — reinforcing the log-vs-power-rule distinction.
