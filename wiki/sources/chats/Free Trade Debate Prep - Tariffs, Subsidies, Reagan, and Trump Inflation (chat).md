# Free Trade Debate Prep - Tariffs, Subsidies, Reagan, and Trump Inflation

Debate-prep session (Dec 2025) in which Traveler arms himself to argue **for free trade against protectionism** in a class debate the next day. It is a tidy snapshot of how he fuses [[IB Economics (SL)]] microeconomics with his [[Political and Economic Views|libertarian-leaning]] moral framework, marrying "ethics + algebra" exactly as he likes to.

## What he asked for
He pre-loaded the model with his own thesis and wanted it sharpened: (1) a **moral argument** that the government has no right to dictate, via tariff, where citizens buy goods; (2) a **graph-based argument** that tariffs offer no guarantee of increased domestic production; (3) the comparative-advantage case that protectionism clings to an "antiquated view of domestic economics"; plus protectionist counterpoints and rebuttals.

## Key content
- **Moral case:** tariffs as coercion ("protectionism asks consumers to subsidize producers without consent"), free trade as poverty-reducing, and the consistency trap of preaching competition then banning it.
- **Graph case:** the standard IB tariff diagram (price rises Pw → Pw+T, consumption falls, inefficient domestic expansion, government revenue rectangle, two **deadweight-loss triangles**); the point that inelastic/constrained supply means domestic output may barely rise; comparative advantage shifting the consumption-possibilities curve outward.
- **Subsidy effect:** follow-up on the subsidy diagram — supply shifts down/right, consumer price falls while producer price rises, quantity expands past the socially efficient level, government expenditure rectangle, and DWL from overproduction. Framed as "tariffs wearing a nicer shirt."
- **Rebuttals** to infant-industry, jobs, national-security, "unfair competition," and "keep money in the country" arguments — including the line "a trade deficit is not a national report card."

## Real-world anchors
He pushed past the theory into empirics, which is characteristic of his data-grounded style (cf. his [[Extended Essay (Economics)]] work on inflation):
- **Reagan's tariffs:** steel VERs (1984), the 45% Harley-Davidson motorcycle tariff (1983), semiconductor retaliation (1987), and continued auto VERs — used as a "even the free-market poster child did it, and it failed" rhetorical weapon.
- **Trump's tariffs and inflation:** near-100% pass-through to consumers/firms per Fed/NBER findings, cost-push pressure visible by 2018 before COVID, reduced competition enabling domestic markups, and retaliation effects.
- **Long-term outlook:** permanently elevated price level, lower productivity growth, sticky supply-chain rigidity, unchanged trade deficit, and no domestic industrial rebirth ("tariffs don't fix the economy, they fossilize it").
- A tangential closing query on the **Apple vs. Samsung** global smartphone split (2025 shipments roughly 19% each), unrelated to the debate.
