# Ten Commandments, Free Will, and Morality Without God (chat)

A long, genuinely personal philosophy session (gpt-5, Sept 2025) in which [[Traveler Stansberry]] reasons aloud through the load-bearing problems of his own worldview: free will, determinism, the grounding of morality, the Euthyphro dilemma, and consciousness. It is one of the clearest first-person windows into his [[Core Convictions]] and the live tensions inside them, and it directly fleshes out his [[Political and Economic Views|self-described]] identity as a determinist and religious skeptic.

## What he was working through

He opens asking whether the Ten Commandments contradict the Christian view of free will, then states his own situation plainly: **"I'm an atheist... I do believe in free will."** That combination is the whole problem. The chat walks him through the standard map — hard determinism, libertarian free will (which smuggles in a soul or new physics), and **compatibilism** (freedom = acting from your own reasons, not coercion) — and pushes him toward compatibilism as the only non-magical option consistent with materialism. This is a direct, sharpened restatement of the [[Fate and Free Will]] theme that recurs across his English coursework, now argued in his own voice rather than through a literary text.

A notable detour: he asks whether AI developing "free will" would require magic or just programming, drawing a deliberate **human-vs-AI parallel** (origins, reasons-responsiveness, self-modification, narrative continuity). The model gives him an operational checklist of markers that would make an AI "free" in the compatibilist sense. This connects his metaphysics straight to his engineering life with [[Homework Hatch (startup)]] and his interest in agentic AI — for Traveler, the free-will question is not abstract; it is adjacent to what he builds. (The chat also reveals his custom ChatGPT "modes" system — 0 Normal, 1 Researcher, 2 Data Analyst, 3 Advice Giver, 4 Coder — and his standing instruction to stop overusing em dashes, saved to memory.)

## The moral core

The back half is the more revealing part. His stated presupposition: the Bible "constantly has proclaimed morality to be true and righteous but has been dead wrong" — citing God commanding genocide of the Amalekites and rules for keeping slaves as blatant contradictions of the commandments themselves. He wants to know whether **moral objectivism survives without God**, and he is steered toward secular foundations (utilitarian well-being, Kantian consistency, virtue ethics, [[The Moral Impact of Slavery|moral realism]], constructivism).

He keeps pulling the thread back to the **Euthyphro dilemma** — is something good because God commands it, or does God command it because it is good — and refuses to let go until he understands *why* "God's nature is goodness itself" collapses into arbitrariness. He tests it personally ("what if I say I am kind?" / "whatever I do is kind") until he sees the point: a moral word with no external standard is empty, whether the speaker is God or himself. He is visibly frustrated by Christian interpretive "gymnastics" — Old vs New Covenant, "God's higher ways," contextualization — calling it "extremely neglectful" that believers read noble verses literally and vile ones as metaphor. He notes he has lost this debate's framing against "top 1% globally IQ" Christians who deploy the "God's higher ways" shield, and wants to understand why it fails as logic even when it works as loyalty.

His landing point, endorsed by the chat: drop biblical inerrancy and divine command theory, **keep morality** but root it in human reason and shared reality. This is [[Self-Reliance]]-flavored individualism applied to ethics — authority rejected, judgment kept internal. See [[Faith vs. Knowledge]].

## Why it matters

This is not coursework; it is Traveler doing philosophy for himself, and it documents the actual reasoning behind labels the wiki already attaches to him. It shows his characteristic move: refuse the comfortable catch-all, demand the mechanism, dumb it down until it is airtight. It also flags consciousness (the "hard problem," qualia, illusionism) as the question he finds *harder* than free will — the one place he concedes a materialist worldview gets genuinely uncomfortable, even floating "so divine creator it is" as a half-serious provocation before retreating. A rare, candid map of where his convictions are firm and where they are still open. See [[Tensions and Open Questions]] and [[Intellectual Interests]].
