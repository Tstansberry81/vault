# Antimatter, Dark Matter, the Eiffel Tower, and The Last Duel

A curiosity-driven session, dated after his [[IB Math SL Paper 1 and 2 Walkthrough - Binomial, Series, and TI-84 Drills (chat)|May exams]], that has nothing to do with assigned coursework — pure intellectual browsing across cosmology, history, and film. [[Traveler Stansberry]] opens by asking about **antimatter, dark matter, and dark energy** and gets a clean disambiguation of the three "dark"-adjacent concepts that pop science routinely conflates: antimatter as lab-made antiparticles that annihilate via $E=mc^2$ (and the baryon-asymmetry puzzle of why the Big Bang left ~1 extra matter particle per billion); dark matter as invisible, gravitationally-detected mass inferred from galaxy rotation curves and lensing (~27% of the universe); and dark energy as the negative-pressure property of space driving accelerating expansion (~68%), tied to Einstein's resurrected cosmological constant $\Lambda$ and the $10^{120}$ vacuum-energy mismatch — "the worst prediction in physics history." This extends the [[IB Physics (HL)]] foundation well past syllabus into the kind of fundamental-physics speculation that recurs in his chats.

He then pivots to who built the **Eiffel Tower and why** (Gustave Eiffel's company, designed by Koechlin and Nouguier for the 1889 Exposition Universelle marking the centenary of the French Revolution; meant to be temporary, saved by its usefulness for radio and military signaling), and finally asks for a breakdown of Ridley Scott's **The Last Duel** (2021). The film discussion is the most thematically loaded: the three-perspective Rashomon structure where only Marguerite's account is titled "The Truth," and the reading of the medieval trial-by-combat as a study of ego, class, institutional injustice, and toxic [[Masculinity]] — "good soldier ≠ good husband."

What it reveals is the breadth of his self-directed [[Intellectual Interests]]: a determinist, [[Faith vs. Knowledge|religiously skeptical]] mind drawn to the unsolved 95% of the cosmos and to art that interrogates power and self-justifying narrative. The Last Duel's concerns with masculinity and [[Power, Hierarchy, and Justice]] echo themes he tracks across his [[English Curriculum 2022-2026|English]] reading. See also his [[Film and TV]] page.
