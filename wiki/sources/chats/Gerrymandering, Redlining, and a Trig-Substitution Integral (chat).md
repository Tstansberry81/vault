# Gerrymandering, Redlining, and a Trig-Substitution Integral

A short mixed-homework session from Feb 2026 that blends US civics/history, [[IB Economics (SL)]], and [[IB Math (SL)]]. [[Traveler Stansberry]] opens with a bare two-word prompt — "jerrymandering and redlining" — and ChatGPT delivers a paired explainer, then pivots to a calculus integral he says he was never taught.

## Gerrymandering and redlining
The explainer frames both as American "political engineering" built on drawing lines on a map and pretending they are neutral. **Gerrymandering**: manipulating district boundaries via *packing* (cramming opposition voters into a few districts) and *cracking* (splitting them so they never form a majority), distorting representation and fueling polarization. Legal context: racial gerrymandering violates the Voting Rights Act, but *Rucho v. Common Cause* (2019) left purely partisan gerrymandering unpoliced by federal courts. **Redlining**: the 1930s Home Owners' Loan Corporation graded neighborhoods A–D, outlining "hazardous" Black/immigrant areas in red, which banks then treated as a no-lend zone — denied mortgages, worse rates, no insurance, public disinvestment. The Fair Housing Act (1968) banned it, but the wealth and spatial effects persist, with Baltimore flagged as a textbook case (relevant to Traveler, who is near Maryland — see his [[Extended Essay (Economics)]] on Maryland inflation).

The thread is explicitly framed for an econ student: redlining is **credit rationing driven by discrimination, not fundamentals** — capital allocation based on race, producing a self-fulfilling prophecy (label risky → starve of capital → values decline → "see, risky"). The compounding wealth gap is "accumulated capital foregone." When Traveler asks "so why did they do this," the answer lists normalized racism baked into underwriting manuals, lazy "homogeneity = safe" risk modeling, white property-value protection, and political incentives. The closing synthesis ties the two together: redlining → lower homeownership → less intergenerational wealth → weaker political influence → easier to gerrymander. "State-backed market manipulation," cast as a critique of capital-allocation efficiency — material that sits at the intersection of his [[Political and Economic Views]] and [[Power, Hierarchy, and Justice]].

## The integral
Traveler then asks to integrate √(16−x²). The model walks the full trig substitution (x = 4sinθ, the cos² identity, back-substitution) to the answer (x/2)√(16−x²) + 8·arcsin(x/4) + C, noting the integrand is the top half of a radius-4 circle (definite integral −4 to 4 = 8π). His reaction — "jesus christ i never leraned how to do that shit" — gets a reassuring "non-traumatic" reply with a memorizable cheat-code formula for √(a²−x²) and a jab that trig substitution may have been skipped in class.
