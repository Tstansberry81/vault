# L'Hopital's Rule, Tariff Welfare Graphs, and a Floor-Function Integral

A grab-bag homework session spanning calculus, [[IB Economics (SL)]], and a *Stranger Things* lore detour. Characteristic of Traveler's pattern of using ChatGPT as a single multi-subject tutor in one sitting, the conversation moves from a quick concept refresher to a genuinely difficult problem he was stuck on.

## L'Hopital's Rule
Opens with a one-word prompt ("lhopitals rule") and a full conceptual walkthrough: the rule applies only to indeterminate forms 0/0 or ∞/∞, you differentiate numerator and denominator separately, and you can reapply it. Covered the standard worked examples (sin x / x = 1, e^x / x^3 → ∞ showing exponential beats polynomial, and the x·ln x = ln x / (1/x) rewrite trick for converting 0·∞ and other illegal forms). Relevant to [[IB Math (SL)]], though L'Hopital sits at the edge of/beyond the SL syllabus, suggesting self-extension into calculus.

## Tariff welfare analysis
The economic core. Traveler asked for the effects of a tariff explained on a graph and in text, already understanding that most of the tariff cost passes to consumers. The model gave the standard import-tariff diagram: price rises from world price Pw to Pw + t, domestic supply expands, demand contracts, imports shrink, government collects a revenue rectangle, and two deadweight-loss triangles appear (overproduction and underconsumption). He then uploaded his own **Chapter 24 steel-market notes** (Pdom = $100, Pw = $60, Pt = $70 tariff) and worked through his teacher's labeled regions a–e and g–k.

The most revealing exchange is Traveler repeatedly pressing on a confusing **yellow region** his teacher marked "NO transactions take place," and on why welfare areas a and b appear to sit "left of the supply curve." The model clarified the conceptual trap most students miss: surplus is a *vertical* value measured between curves, not a *horizontal* quantity of units produced — shading always extends back to the axis as a convention, and nothing is ever produced left of the supply curve. His persistence here (four follow-up questions drilling the same diagram) shows he refuses to accept a graph he can't fully reconstruct from first principles, consistent with his analytical bent in [[Intellectual Profile]].

## Floor-function integral
Closes with a hard competition-style problem he photographed: an integral over [0,1] of an expression containing ⌊1/x⌋. The trick is recognizing the integrand is piecewise constant on intervals (1/(n+1), 1/n], where ⌊1/x⌋ = n. Splitting the integral, the interval length 1/(n(n+1)) cancels the n(n+1) in the numerator, collapsing everything to half the alternating harmonic series, giving the answer **(1/2)·ln 2**. This is well above SL level and signals the kind of math he gravitates toward, connecting to the [[UVA and the Quant Question]] and his math-minor ambitions.
