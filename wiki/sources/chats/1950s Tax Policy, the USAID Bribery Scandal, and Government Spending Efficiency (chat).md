# 1950s Tax Policy, the USAID Bribery Scandal, and Government Spending Efficiency

A short, three-question informational chat in which Traveler asked ChatGPT for quick explainers on (1) US tax policy in the 1950s, (2) a recent USAID bribery scandal, and (3) what USAID actually does and whether it benefited the US. The exchange sits at the intersection of his [[IB History (HL)]] and [[IB Economics (SL)]] coursework, and the model explicitly tried to tie each topic back to his [[Extended Essay (Economics)]] on fiscal policy and inflation.

## 1950s tax policy
The model walked through the era's headline numbers: a top marginal income tax rate of **91%** (on income over ~$200,000, roughly $2.4M today), a corporate rate near **52%**, capital gains around 25%, and heavy excise taxes on alcohol, tobacco, and cars. Key framing for an economics/history student: despite punishing nominal rates, **effective** rates were far lower thanks to deductions and loopholes, and total tax revenue still sat at ~18-20% of GDP, comparable to today. It situated this in the Eisenhower balancing act — fiscal conservatism via balanced budgets plus large infrastructure (Interstate Highway System) and defense spending — and the Revenue Act of 1954 / Internal Revenue Code of 1954. The takeaway ("socialism on paper, capitalism in practice") connects to his interest in macro [[Political and Economic Views]] and supply-side debates.

## USAID scandal and mission
The chat then covered a contracting-officer bribery scheme (Roderick Watson et al.) involving 14+ prime contracts and ~$550M in improperly awarded, taxpayer-funded contracts, followed by an overview of USAID's purpose since its 1961 founding under JFK — humanitarian aid, health programs (PEPFAR), Cold War-era soft power, and the geopolitical motive of preventing impoverished nations from turning communist.

## Relevance to his work
The model repeatedly pivoted to his [[Extended Essay (Economics)]], suggesting the USAID corruption case as a worked example of **waste and misallocation in government spending**: how leakage into corruption reduces the fiscal multiplier and weakens the link between public spending and macro outcomes (growth, inflation, welfare). This is a small but telling instance of Traveler using ChatGPT as a fast briefing tool across his [[IB History (HL)]] / [[IB Economics (SL)]] subjects rather than for graded output. Lightweight relative to his deeper EE research chats, but it reinforces his recurring focus on fiscal policy efficiency and public-choice angles.
