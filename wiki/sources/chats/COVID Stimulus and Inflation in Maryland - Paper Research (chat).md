# COVID Stimulus and Inflation in Maryland - Paper Research

A short, three-turn research-gathering session in which Traveler asks ChatGPT (gpt-4o, with a "ScholarGPT" research persona) to find papers and data for a research paper on **the effects of COVID-19 policy (specifically stimulus bills) on inflation in Maryland**. The chat is purely a sourcing exercise: assemble a bibliography, summarize it, and format citations.

## What he was doing
1. **Find sources** — he asked for "papers and data" on COVID stimulus causing inflation in Maryland. The model returned ~10 academic papers (Chetty et al.'s real-time spending tracker, Ball/Leigh/Mishra's *Understanding US Inflation During the COVID-19 Era*, Dynan & Elmendorf, Auerbach et al.'s *Fiscal Effects of COVID-19*, Lin on stimulus and housing inflation, Viitanen's monetarist money-supply analysis, etc.) plus Maryland data sources (MD Dept. of Labor CPI, BEA state personal income, Richmond Fed regional reports).
2. **Summarize** — he requested a 300-word synthesis of all the papers. The takeaway: no paper isolates Maryland exclusively, but the state tracks national patterns — demand-side stimulus (checks, enhanced UI) overwhelmed supply chains and inflated housing and services prices.
3. **Citations** — he asked for MLA citations for all the papers.

## What it reveals
This is the raw-materials stage of a macroeconomics research paper that applies textbook demand-pull / fiscal-policy theory to a real regional case. The framing — stimulus as a demand-side shock outrunning aggregate supply — is exactly the [[IB Economics (SL)]] machinery (AD/AS, fiscal policy, the [[Quantitative Theory of Money]]) he works through elsewhere. It overlaps thematically with his other pandemic-economics chats and connects to his broader interest in inflation, money supply, and policy.

> [!warning] Source-reliability caveat
> The "ScholarGPT" persona that generated these citations is the kind of LLM tool prone to fabricating papers and URLs (several links point to upload/delivery endpoints rather than canonical DOIs). Some entries — e.g. "Yacob (2024)" and "Osuoha (2023)" claiming Maryland-specific data — should be independently verified before citing in an academic paper.
