# Economic Impact of COVID-19 - EE Self-Grading and Wage-Price Spiral

A working-session chat in which Traveler pastes a near-complete draft of his [[Extended Essay (Economics)]] into ChatGPT and asks it to grade the essay "as is, like an IB Diploma grader," then to critique it without rewriting, then to clarify a contradiction in its two scores, then to explain a specific concept (the wage-price spiral). It is one of the clearest windows into the actual content and editing of his EE.

## The essay draft
The pasted text is the full body of his EE on **U.S. pandemic fiscal and monetary policy as a driver of inflation, with Maryland (Baltimore-Columbia-Towson CPI-U) as the local case study** — the same project documented in [[Economics EE - Pandemic Fiscal Policy and Maryland Inflation (chat)|Economics EE - Pandemic Fiscal Policy and Maryland Inflation]] and [[COVID Stimulus and Inflation in Maryland - Paper Research (chat)|COVID Stimulus and Inflation in Maryland - Paper Research]]. The thesis: COVID's "reactionary fiscal and monetary policy" caused major inflation in 2022 (minor in 2021/2023) via excessive aggregate demand, rising debt, falling interest rates, gas prices, and a tightening labor market. Key evidence marshaled in the draft:
- **CARES Act + ARPA** injected ~$4 trillion (~20% of a ~$20T economy) of demand into a supply-constrained economy — demand-pull layered on cost-push.
- Maryland CPI-U tracked national: rose from ~261.5 (Dec 2020) to ~306.5 (June 2022, the peak), a ~6.3% local change vs ~6.5% national.
- Fed dropped the EFFR from 1.58% (Feb 2020) to ~0.05% (April 2020) under Jerome Powell; low rates plus low supply fed inflation.
- Maryland-specific: Hogan's 2021 RELIEF Act (~$1B), rising state debt ($9.5B→$10.7B 2020-22), gas peaking at $5/gal (June 2022), and a JEC figure that the average MD household paid $1,246/month more by 2024 vs Jan 2021.
- Counters weighed: supply-chain/Ukraine-war cost-push (Biden's narrative) vs MIT Sloan's Mark Kritzman, who found ~42% of inflation attributable to federal spending — used to argue demand-pull dominated.
- A labor-market-tightness section (discouraged workers, unemployment benefits, MD labor participation rate falling to 64.7% by July 2025) that he himself flags as minor.

## What the grading revealed
ChatGPT first scored it against the real EE rubric (Criteria A–E, max 34): **~21/34, a predicted C/borderline B**, docking hard on placeholder "Graph:" labels with no actual figures, inconsistent MLA citations, missing word count, and conversational tone ("the economy was in shambles, and everyone was panicking"). When asked again to "rip it apart," it produced a far more generous ~37/47 (predicted A/high B) — using a non-standard rubric. Traveler caught the inconsistency ("whyd you also say this about the exact same essay") and pinned it down: the higher score assumed the essay *after* the recommended edits. Top fixes flagged: state the research question explicitly, insert real captioned graphs (AD/AS shifts, CPI trends, gas vs CPI), clean MLA, make Maryland the centerpiece rather than a tack-on, cut the labor-participation filler, and kill run-on sentences.

## Wage-price spiral question
He closes by asking what a wage-price spiral is, whether it happened in Maryland, and for a graph. The answer: it is the self-reinforcing prices→wage-demands→prices feedback loop ("built-in"/wage-push inflation), but mostly a theoretical risk — the IMF, CRS, and OCC all found **no decisively sustained wage-price spiral** in the U.S. or Maryland over 2020–23, only spiral-like elements. A useful caution for his draft, which had asserted wage-price spirals could "quickly get out of hand."
