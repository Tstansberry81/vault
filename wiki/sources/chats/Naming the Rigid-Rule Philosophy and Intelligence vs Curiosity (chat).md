# Naming the Rigid-Rule Philosophy and Intelligence vs Curiosity (chat)

A short, tip-of-the-tongue chat in which [[Traveler Stansberry]] tries to recall the name of "the philosophy that defines rules [as having] to be rigid," then drifts into a metacognitive question about whether intelligence breeds curiosity. It reads as casual but maps cleanly onto his [[Theory of Knowledge]] instincts and his recurring interest in [[Fate and Free Will]], moral structure, and his own [[Intellectual Interests]].

## The label hunt
Traveler works toward the term through successive refinements, each time narrowing the target:
- "rules have to be rigid" → **deontology** / Kant's categorical imperative, contrasted with utilitarianism, moral relativism, and virtue ethics.
- "tied to government structure" → **Legalism** (ancient Chinese, Qin Shi Huang / Qin dynasty: clear laws, harsh punishment, no flexibility), authoritarianism, absolutism.
- "no moral exceptions to committing crimes" → **moral absolutism applied to law** — intent and context don't save you.
- He poses the classic dilemma: a man stealing an apple **because he can** vs. **to feed his starving kids**. The model splits the verdict across moral absolutism (both wrong), strict deontology (both wrong, universalizability), utilitarianism (case 2 justified), and virtue ethics (character over rule), then notes real legal systems use *mens rea*, necessity defenses, and mitigating circumstances.
- Traveler finally guesses **"realism"** — but is corrected: **legal realism** (Oliver Wendell Holmes Jr.) is the *opposite* of rigid rule-worship; the rigid mechanical-application label he wanted is closer to **legal formalism**, alongside moral absolutism and strict deontology.

This apple-thief framing (act vs. intention, structure vs. compassion) is the same law-and-justice tension that runs through his [[Power, Hierarchy, and Justice]] reading and his [[IB History (HL)]] work; the "rigid system gives stability, flexible system gives humanity" trade-off is squarely [[Individual vs. Society]] territory.

## The pivot: does a better brain want to know more?
He abruptly asks whether "the better your brain is at processing things the more you want to know." The answer threads intelligence, dopamine/novelty-seeking, and tolerance for ambiguity — concluding processing power and curiosity are correlated but not causal (you can be smart and incurious; temperament and reward sensitivity matter). The "dangerous combination" named — high intelligence + high curiosity + low fear of uncertainty — is flagged as "sounding a little familiar," a pointed nod to Traveler's own profile as a [[determinist]] skeptic with broad [[Intellectual Interests]]. It pairs with his self-aware, self-diagnosing streak documented across [[Intellectual Profile]] and [[Emotional Life and Inner World]].

Lightweight and exploratory rather than coursework-bound, but a clean snapshot of how he reasons about ethics: he reaches for the formal vocabulary (deontology, absolutism, formalism) and tests it against a concrete moral hypothetical before turning the lens on himself.
