# EE Submission Tips - Maryland Labor Market Tightness and Federal Debt

A long, late-stage editing session (gpt-5, Sept 2025) on Traveler's [[Extended Essay (Economics)]], opened with the triumphant line "whole essay is done for ee." It is the most complete window the vault has into the actual *text* of the EE: he pastes the full draft and asks for ranking, line edits, source verification, and help shoring up the two weakest arguments. The work directly extends [[IB Economics (SL)]] and the [[Economics EE - Pandemic Fiscal Policy and Maryland Inflation (chat)|Economics EE - Pandemic Fiscal Policy and Maryland Inflation]] research thread.

## The essay and the verdict
The EE argues that the U.S. government's COVID-era fiscal and monetary response (CARES Act, ARPA, near-zero EFFR) drove inflation peaking in 2022, using the **Baltimore-Columbia-Towson CPI-U** as a Maryland case study against national figures. ChatGPT's mock examiner score: **~37/47 → predicted A or high B**, with full marks on knowledge/understanding but only **2/4 on presentation** (placeholder "Graph:" markers with no actual figures, no word count, inconsistent MLA in-text citations mixing "para.", page numbers, and bare "1"). The diagnosed core flaw: he documents national policy in depth but never directly answers *"to what extent"* Maryland was affected — the precise evaluative move IB rewards. This shows Traveler is comfortable marshaling a [[Power, Hierarchy, and Justice|policy]] argument with data but tends to under-deliver the explicit comparative judgment.

## The "extent" fix and the debt math
The richest stretch is Traveler working out a clean national-vs-Maryland debt comparison. He resists ChatGPT's first hand-wavy framing ("proportionally large for a state budget") and corrects it himself — noting the $4T didn't all land in 2020, that ARPA passed in 2021, and that the comparison year is 2021. He pushes for real FRED figures (Q4 2021 national debt ≈ $29.4T) and lands on a defensible pair: Maryland's RELIEF Act (~$1B) ≈ **10.2%** of state debt, vs CARES+ARPA outlays realized in 2021 (CBO's ~$2.3T) ≈ **8.2%** of ~$28T federal debt. He even catches a "billion/trillion" denominator typo and parses raw CBO language ("reduced projected revenue by $0.4T / 10%, increased outlays by $2.3T / almost 50%"). The episode shows genuine quantitative rigor and source-skepticism — he refuses to let the model overclaim and insists on apples-to-apples math.

## Source verification and the labor-market argument
Traveler flags a stat he couldn't re-locate in his notes ("68% more recipients... earned more not working") and asks ChatGPT to find it rather than fabricate — it traces a likely conflation between a U.S. Chamber "61%" figure and a Fee.org/NBER "68%" replacement-rate study, and he's warned not to over-quote. Most of the session then rebuilds his weakest section (Argument 4, the labor market). He learns and applies the **wage-price spiral** mechanism, **labor market tightness**, and the **unemployed-to-job-openings (U/V) ratio** as a clean tightness measure — pulling the FRED chart himself and noting the ratio stayed below 1 from April 2021 to June 2024 (0.6–0.74 in 2023). ChatGPT repeatedly flags his casual register ("kids back in school without masks," "everyone was panicking," "decimated") as too op-ed for an EE.
