# Trump Corporate Tax Policy Analysis - Advisor Board Project

A research session for a school "economic advisor board" project in which Traveler set out to **diagnose and critique current Trump-administration corporate-tax and deregulation policy** in a deliberately non-partisan, analysis-only frame. He explicitly tied the assignment back to his [[IB Economics (SL)]] class: the project's whole point is *policy analysis*, the exact skill his classmates struggled with on their recent IAs and where (he notes) he "likely got the highest grade on the class." This is [[Political and Economic Views]] meeting coursework — supply-side mechanics rendered through standard IB tooling.

## The framework he imposed

Traveler structured the entire inquiry around four fixed questions applied to each policy, refusing to evaluate effectiveness or real-world outcomes — only *stated intent and design logic*:
1. What problem does the administration believe exists?
2. What is the objective of the policy?
3. How is the policy supposed to work (mechanism)?
4. What trade-offs is the administration willing to accept?

He drove the chat hard toward deliverables: "enough information to fill 4 PowerPoint slides + two graphs," four named policies, direct primary sources, and actual administration quotes — repeatedly pushing back ("still need links," "NEED SOURCES," "NO trump and bidens stance") when answers were too vague or off-target. This is a recurring pattern in his work: he treats the model as a sourcing-and-structuring engine, not an oracle.

## The four policies analyzed

All four are anchored in the **One Big Beautiful Bill Act (Public Law 119-21, signed July 4, 2025)** and a January 2025 deregulation executive order:
- **Permanent 100% bonus depreciation** (full immediate expensing of qualifying capital) — lowers the user cost of capital, shifts AD right short-run and LRAS right long-run.
- **10-to-1 corporate deregulation** (remove ten regulations per new one) — treats regulation as an implicit per-unit cost, shifts SRAS right.
- **Rejection of OECD Pillar Two** (the 15% global minimum tax / "top-up" mechanism) — a "boundary-setting" sovereignty policy framed as the "international lock" protecting the other three domestic incentives from foreign clawback. Treasury's "no force or effect" statement is the keystone source.
- **Business tax-base adjustments** — Section 174 domestic R&D expensing and Section 163(j) interest-deduction (EBITDA-based) changes.

A useful conceptual distinction he extracted: these are **tax-*base* policies, not tax-*rate* policies** — they redefine "21% of *what*," not the 21% itself. He also got a clean standalone primer on what 100% bonus depreciation is ("write it all off immediately"; timing of deductions, not total deductions), with a $1M-machine numerical example slotting straight into a slide.

## The Trump vs. Biden pivot

The session's analytical payoff came when Traveler redirected from a Trump-internal "domestic vs. international regulation" comparison to a **Trump vs. Biden corporate-regulation contrast**. The model produced a six-row comparison table: regulation as *cost burden* (overregulation problem, deregulate for competitiveness, SRAS-right, accepts higher externality risk) versus regulation as *market correction* (market-failure/power-concentration problem, oversight and antitrust, accepts higher compliance costs). This maps cleanly onto the broader [[Individual vs. Society]] and government-role tensions in his [[Political and Economic Views]], and onto the macro-schools material in his [[Theory of Knowledge]]-adjacent econ work.

The whole exercise is a strong artifact of his econ analytical method: distinguish rate from base, link each tool to an AD-AS or labor-market transmission channel, name trade-offs without moralizing, and demand primary government sources (IRS, Treasury, White House fact sheets) over "partisan think-tank sludge." It reinforces the supply-side/deregulatory lean already visible across his [[Extended Essay (Economics)]] and free-trade/tariff coursework, while showing him insisting on the non-partisan analytical discipline IB rewards.
