# Morality, Policy, and the Role of Government - Civic Engagement Club Debrief

A long, free-ranging political-philosophy conversation in which [[Traveler Stansberry]] debriefs ChatGPT after a meeting of the school **Civic Engagement Club** he presides over at [[St. Paul's School]]. It is one of the clearest windows into his [[Political and Economic Views]] and his self-described "a little right leaning" / libertarian instincts, and it documents him road-testing arguments rather than just asking questions. The chat opens with the prompt that gives it its title — *"should moral values guide policy from government?"* — and spirals through theocracy, cultural appropriation, free speech, race, crime, welfare, foreign policy, income inequality, and competing forms of government.

## The club and how he runs it
He frames the club as a rare space where juniors and seniors "talk like adults," inviting disagreement and civil discourse. He notes the room is roughly **70/30 right-to-left** (attributing the skew to wealthy Maryland lacrosse communities) while the faculty advisor and administration lean left. His pedagogical move — get students to define **what they think the role of government *should* be** rather than trade moral slogans — recurs as his signature framing. He dismisses both extremes of youth politics ("half the room thinks Nick Fuentes is god, the other half thinks Mamdani isn't a socialist"). This connects to his [[Extracurriculars and Achievements]] alongside [[Investment Club]] and [[Coding Club]].

## Core arguments he advances
- **Morality shouldn't guide policy** because morality is subjective; "wrong morals = wrong policy." His anchor example: the Bible's instructions on treating slaves mean a US theocracy could be morally bound to uphold slavery — theocracy as a *stress test* for moral governance. Ties directly to his religious skepticism (see [[Faith vs. Knowledge]], [[Political and Economic Views]]).
- **Free expression over offense.** On white students wearing durags at Halloween — flagged by the school's DEI head as appropriation — he argues the school has no moral legitimacy to ban a cultural item from one group while permitting other headwear; *offense* should not restrict expression, only *violence* should. ChatGPT names this the **John Stuart Mill harm principle** and ties it to *Tinker* and *Brandenburg v. Ohio*. He concedes the school-as-managed-community "hostile environment" counterargument but rejects hate-speech regulation in the broader society.
- **Cancel culture** as "lateral tyranny" / crowd-sourced moral policing with no due process.

## Topics surveyed
He works through CRT (diagnostic, not inherently racist; the "racism = prejudice + power" vs. "racism = belief in racial hierarchy" definitional split), whether anyone can be racist, Baltimore crime (he lives nearby; the answer points to redlining, deindustrialization, and one-party stagnation over "culture"), "defund the police"/BLM scandals, and the rise of politics-as-identity since the Obama years.

## Economics and foreign policy
He recounts arguing welfare-cuts vs. the ~$300B spent in Ukraine with a teacher, invoking **Washington's Farewell Address** against entangling alliances (noting "yeah, I got a 5 on APUSH"). On **income inequality** he pushes back usefully: *wouldn't we expect the top 20% to earn a disproportionate share?* — eliciting the "efficient vs. excessive inequality" distinction (US top quintile holds ~51.9% of income vs. a healthier ~40-45%). This dovetails with his economics coursework in [[IB Economics (SL)]] and the [[Extended Essay (Economics)]].

## Defining a person's politics in one question
He prizes his "role of government" question as an ideology litmus test; ChatGPT counters with *"Who do you trust more to make moral and practical decisions: individuals or institutions?"* and notes **state vs. federal** is the American version of the same axis. The chat closes with a tour of rival systems to the democratic-republic (technocracy, Singapore-style meritocracy, benevolent authoritarianism, libertarian minimal state, theocracy, direct/digital democracy) and why socialism/communism keep failing in practice — the information problem, incentive collapse, human nature — possibly viable only in a post-scarcity, AI-managed future.

This source strengthens his profile as an [[Individual vs. Society]]-oriented [[Intellectual Profile|individualist and determinist]] who reasons from first principles about [[Power, Hierarchy, and Justice]] and is comfortable defending unpopular positions civilly.
