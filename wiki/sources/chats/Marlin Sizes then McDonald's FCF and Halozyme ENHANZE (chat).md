## Marlin (the throwaway part)
Two quick questions on blue marlin (~11 ft, 200–400 lb typical; females to ~14 ft and near 2,000 lb) and white marlin (the smallest major marlin, ~6–7 ft, ~60 lb). No deeper purpose — pure curiosity, likely sportfishing-adjacent.

## McDonald's free cash flow vs. competitors
The real meat. He asks for MCD free cash flow charted against Burger King, then against its "biggest FCF competitor in the industry" over 10 years. Key takeaways the model surfaced:
- **MCD FCF** is steady and strong, roughly $3.8B–$7.4B over 2010–2024, peaking near $7.2–7.4B in 2021 and 2023; ~+4.9% 5-yr CAGR.
- **Burger King (via Restaurant Brands Intl, ticker QSR)** runs 5–7× smaller (~$1.1–1.3B TTM) and is shrinking (~-4% 5-yr CAGR).
- **Starbucks (SBUX)** is named the largest FCF peer — far more volatile (negative in 2014, ~$10B spike in 2018, near-zero in 2020).

This is classic [[Traveler Stansberry]] fundamental screening: isolating a single quality metric (FCF) and ranking peers on it, the same instinct behind his custom stock-ranking projects and Bloomberg-driven research.

## Halozyme / ENHANZE
He asks who Halozyme sells its ENHANZE technology to. The answer clarifies the business model: Halozyme does not sell a drug — it **licenses** its rHuPH20 hyaluronidase enzyme (enabling subcutaneous instead of IV delivery) to big pharma (J&J/Janssen, Roche/Chugai, BMS, argenx, Pfizer, AbbVie, Lilly, Takeda, etc.) and collects milestones + royalties. A royalty-licensing "platform tax" model — exactly the kind of moaty, high-margin business he tends to favor in his qualitative screening.
