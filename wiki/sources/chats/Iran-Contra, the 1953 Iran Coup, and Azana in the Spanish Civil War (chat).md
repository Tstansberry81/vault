# Iran-Contra, the 1953 Iran Coup, and Azana in the Spanish Civil War

A focused [[coursework-history|history]] review session by [[Traveler Stansberry]], working through several discrete [[IB History (HL)]] topics in rapid succession — covert foreign policy, US-Iran relations, and the Spanish Civil War. No drift into other subjects; this is pure exam-prep fact-gathering.

He started with the **Iran-Contra affair** (mid-1980s, Reagan): the two secret operations — selling arms to embargoed Iran (partly to free Lebanese hostages) and diverting the proceeds to fund the Nicaraguan Contras in violation of the Boland Amendment — plus key figures (Oliver North, John Poindexter, Reagan's contested deniability), the 1986 exposure, and its significance as a case of unchecked executive power and covert-operation risk. He then pivoted to the **1953 Iranian coup** (correcting his own "1954"): the CIA/MI6 overthrow of democratically elected PM Mohammad Mossadegh (Operations Ajax/Boot) after his nationalization of Iranian oil, driven by British oil interests and Cold War containment, reinstalling the authoritarian Shah Mohammad Reza Pahlavi.

The strongest analytical moment came when he asked how the coup **ties into the Iranian Revolution** — getting a clean causal chain: the 1953 coup destroyed Iranian trust and installed a repressive, SAVAK-backed Shah → mounting repression and cultural backlash → US-as-villain framing → the 1979 revolution under Khomeini → the embassy seizure and hostage crisis. This is a textbook example of foreign intervention backfiring, useful for essays on [[Power, Hierarchy, and Justice]] and the limits of regime change.

He closed by reaching for **Manuel Azaña** in the **Spanish Civil War** — the liberal-republican President of the Second Republic, his secularizing/military/land reforms that alienated conservatives without satisfying the far left, his struggle to coordinate a fractured Republican coalition against Franco's unified Nationalists (backed by Germany and Italy while Britain and France abstained), and his exile and 1940 death. The framing — moderate reform failing under extreme polarization — is the kind of structured cause-and-effect comparison IB rewards, and it dovetails with his recurring [[Individual vs. Society]] interest in how divided systems collapse. A tight, efficient session with the model's usual sardonic asides ("history doesn't do partial credit").
