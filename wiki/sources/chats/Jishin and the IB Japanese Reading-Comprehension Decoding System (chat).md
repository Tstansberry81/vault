# Jishin and the IB Japanese Reading-Comprehension Decoding System

[[Traveler Stansberry]] works through an [[IB Japanese (SL)]] reading-comprehension worksheet, starting with vocabulary disambiguation — じしん (jishin: 自信 confidence / 地震 earthquake / 自身 oneself), ついて (attached 付いて / arrived 着いて / the academic 〜について "about" / ついていく "to follow"), 話をします ("to have a conversation," nuanced against 話します "to speak"), and しぜん (自然, nature). He then photographs a multi-paragraph passage about a Japanese school's disaster-and-civics curriculum and asks the model to answer the attached questions.

The chat is essentially a tutorial in IB reading-test tactics rather than the language itself. The model walks the question set: a multiple-choice item where the correct answer is C **and** D (geography 山や川 + weather つゆ・たいふう), which [[Traveler Stansberry|Traveler]] catches by saying "there's two options i think"; a block of "find the word in the text" items where the trap is synonym-matching (しにました → text's なくなりました; すぐに → 早く); and short-answer/fill-in items pulled from the passage (じしんのときのあんぜんな場所, 新聞を作ります, ちきゅうのもんだい, ボランティア).

His questions get sharp and verification-oriented — "what is nitsuite in the context of the answer to 25," "for 25 where r u seeing the first two kanji in your answer" — pushing the model to point to the exact passage line (新聞を作ることもあります) and admit the answer is inferential rather than explicit. That refusal to take an AI answer on faith is the same skeptical, "show me the source" habit visible in [[Naming the Rigid-Rule Philosophy and Intelligence vs Curiosity (chat)|Naming the Rigid-Rule Philosophy and Intelligence vs Curiosity]] and his [[Intellectual Interests]]. The session ends with a distilled strategy: read the question first, scan don't deep-read, match meaning not wording, copy from text whenever possible, and treat the exam as structured information decoding under pressure rather than a test of speaking ability.
