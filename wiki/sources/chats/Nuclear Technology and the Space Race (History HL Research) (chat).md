## Core thesis he extracted
The Space Race was built on nuclear-weapons *delivery* infrastructure, not nuclear propulsion: every early launch vehicle (U.S. **Atlas**, **Titan**; Soviet **R-7 Semyorka**) began as an ICBM for warheads. No one detonated a bomb to reach orbit — but ICBM rocketry, guidance, staging, and reentry tech (built to throw nukes) is exactly what reached space. The same R-7 that was the world's first ICBM (1957) launched Sputnik 1 and Gagarin's Vostok 1.

## Topics covered in sequence
- **Nuclear pulse / thermonuclear propulsion** — Project Orion (1958–65): detonate shaped nuclear charges behind a pusher plate; Isp ~6,000 s (vs ~860 s for nuclear-thermal), ~7× the delta-v. Reached subscale chemical-explosive mockups only; killed by the **1963 Partial Test Ban Treaty (PTBT)** and lost DoD sponsorship. ~$11M over 7 years.
- **Nuclear-thermal (fission) rockets** — Project Rover / NERVA (1955–72): reactor heats liquid hydrogen, Isp ~800–900 s. First successful NERVA-class test = **NRX-A2, 24 Sep 1964**, Nevada; XE-Prime long-duration firings by 1969; cancelled Jan 1973 (~$1.4B total). Soviet analog: RD-0410, ground-tested at Semipalatinsk.
- **Treaty constraints** — PTBT (1963) bans nuclear explosions in atmosphere/space/underwater (dooming Orion flight tests); Outer Space Treaty (1967) bars WMDs in orbit.
- **Sputnik 1** — full technical specs (83.6 kg, 58 cm Al-Mg-Ti sphere, 20.005/40.002 MHz beacons, 96.2-min orbit) plus the "Sputnik Crisis," missile-gap panic, creation of NASA (1958), and the **National Defense Education Act (1958)** STEM funding surge.
- **End of the Space Race** — three candidate endpoints argued: Apollo 11 (1969), Apollo-Soyuz (1975, the "handshake in space"), Soviet collapse (1991).
- **Post-NERVA continuity** — SNAP-10A reactor in orbit (1965), Soviet RORSAT reactors, Project Timberwind/SNTP (1987–94), and modern DRACO / Kilopower revival.
- **Social implications** (mode 0) — nuclear fear as the engine of Cold War science policy, the "nuclear sublime," *Dr. Strangelove*, propaganda, and STEM patriotism — followed by a demand for citeable sources (Harvard Gazette, CSIS, NSF history, NASA/Schmidt on Orion).
