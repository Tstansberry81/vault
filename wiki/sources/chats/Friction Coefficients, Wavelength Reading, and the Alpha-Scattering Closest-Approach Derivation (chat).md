# Friction Coefficients, Wavelength Reading, and the Alpha-Scattering Closest-Approach Derivation

A mixed [[IB Physics (HL)]] homework-help session ([[ChatGPT]], gpt-5) where [[Traveler Stansberry]] cycles through several syllabus areas, photographing problems for worked solutions. It opens with **static vs. kinetic friction** ($\mu_s > \mu_k$, static is variable up to a maximum, kinetic is constant) and the five **SUVAT kinematic equations**, then an applied friction-and-stopping-distance problem (box decelerating to rest in ~1.1 m once the applied force is removed). He also gets a full sweep of the **ideal gas law and the four thermodynamic processes** (isothermal/Boyle, isobaric/Charles, isochoric/Gay-Lussac, adiabatic), with the first law $\Delta U = Q - W$ and the Kelvin-only warning.

A long, candid stretch covers **reading wavelength off a graph**. Traveler repeatedly disputes the answer — guessing 6.8, then .68, insisting the wavelength ends "after 0.5 but before 0.7" — and the assistant keeps redirecting him to the count-full-waves method ($\lambda = 4/5 = 0.8$ m). It is a useful glimpse of him stress-testing his own reading against the model rather than accepting it, even when he's wrong.

The most substantive content is **nuclear scattering and energy conversion**. He works through alpha decay of $^{226}$Ra using mass defect and the $1$ u $= 931.5$ MeV/$c^2$ shortcut (~4.9 MeV), then a closest-approach problem equating alpha kinetic energy to Coulomb potential using $ke^2 = 1.44$ MeV·fm — and crucially asks *where the 1.44 comes from*, getting a full SI-units derivation (Coulomb's constant times $e^2$, converted J→MeV and m→fm). When he notes his data booklet lacks the eV→J conversion, he's told to stay in MeV and use $E=qV$ to recover it. The session closes with the nuclear-radius ratio $R \propto A^{1/3}$ giving $R_{Au}/R_{Al} \approx 1.9$.
