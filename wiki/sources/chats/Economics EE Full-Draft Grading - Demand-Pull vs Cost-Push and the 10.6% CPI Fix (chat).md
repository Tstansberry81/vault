# Economics EE Full-Draft Grading - Demand-Pull vs Cost-Push and the 10.6% CPI Fix

Traveler pasted the **entire** working draft of his [[Extended Essay (Economics)]] into ChatGPT (GPT-5, 28 Sep 2025) and asked it to "rate and analyze this extended essay," then bluntly, "is it good enough to pass." The essay is his May 2026 submission (Candidate 3915): *"To what extent did demand-pull and cost-push factors contribute to inflation [...] in Maryland from 2020-2025?"* This chat is the single fullest snapshot of the EE's actual argument and prose in the vault — most other EE chats are narrower (research, data sourcing, examiner-criteria, MLA cleanup).

## The argument, in full
The thesis: pandemic fiscal/monetary intervention drove Maryland inflation (peaking June 2022) chiefly through **demand-pull**, amplified by **cost-push** energy shocks. He defines "Maryland" operationally as the BLS **Baltimore-Columbia-Towson CPI-U** (no statewide series exists). Five channels structure the body:
- **Transfer payments / fiscal stimulus** — CARES ($2.2T) + ARPA ($1.9T) + Maryland's own RELIEF Act 2021 (~$1B, $270M in checks).
- **Debt loads** — federal debt $22.7T → $31.4T (+35%); MD state debt $9.5B → $10.7B.
- **Interest rates** — EFFR collapse from 1.58% to ~0.05% in spring 2020.
- **Energy / cost-push** — Brent crude, the Russia-Ukraine invasion, $5/gal gas, 41.6% YoY energy inflation, distributional burden on low-income households (15% of income).
- **Labor-market tightness** — unemployed-to-openings ratio <1 for three years, MD LFPR falling to ~64.7%, wage-price spiral risk.

He leans on the [[IB Economics (SL)]] toolkit — AD-AS, money market, and labor-market diagrams (Figures 1-5) — and cites the **Kritzman/MIT Sloan** finding that ~42% of the 2022 inflation spike traces to federal spending, plus the NBER pandemic-inflation paper. This connects directly to the data-sourcing and argument-building done in the sibling chats *Economics EE Four Arguments and BLS-FRED Data Sourcing*, *COVID Inflation Data Sourcing - BLS CPI and Kritzman Federal Spending*, and *EE Submission Tips - Maryland Labor Market Tightness and Federal Debt*.

## The verdict
GPT graded it **B/B+ as-is, A- territory with fixes** (~22-25/34), and confirmed "yes, this would pass." The single most important catch: a **self-contradiction in the headline number** — the essay says Maryland inflation "peaked at over 10%" in the intro but "6.3%"/"6%" later. The correct Baltimore CPI-U figure is **10.6% YoY in June 2022** (vs 9.1% national), and the model told him to standardize it everywhere "before an examiner does it for you." Other flagged fixes: add a 2-sentence method box, define the unemployed-to-openings ratio, soften "debt causes inflation" to the conditional IMF framing, sharpen the Brent oil pre- vs post-invasion timing, and restructure each channel **Maryland-first, national-second**. It praised his dual-shock framing, the MD-specific RELIEF Act angle, and the distributional (low-income energy burden) analysis.

## What it reveals
This is Traveler the rigorous empiricist: a determinist, [[Political and Economic Views|fiscally skeptical]] thinker building a heavily-sourced, ~30-source, data-anchored case that *government overreach* (not pure supply chains — explicitly rebutting Biden's framing) caused the inflation. The work feeds his [[UVA and the Quant Question|finance trajectory]] and overlaps his Investment Club interest in macro. It also shows his characteristic anxiety-driven workflow — submitting a near-final draft at the eleventh hour and asking flatly whether it passes — and his comfort using AI as an adversarial examiner, the same instinct behind [[Homework Hatch (startup)]].
