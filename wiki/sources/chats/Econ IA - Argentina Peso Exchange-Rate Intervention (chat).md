# Econ IA - Argentina Peso Exchange-Rate Intervention (chat)

A long, winding working session (24 Feb 2026, GPT-5) in which Traveler builds his **International Economics commentary** for the [[IB Economics (SL)]] [[Individual Oral (IO)|Internal Assessment]]. It is a useful window into how he actually grinds out coursework: start broad, chase several dead ends, then lock onto a defensible topic and engineer the essay backward from the IB rubric.

## The arc: three topics, two abandoned

The chat opens with generic IA mechanics — three 750-word commentaries (micro, macro, global), each pinned to a real news article, diagram-driven, evaluation-heavy. ChatGPT repeatedly flatters his ceiling ("You're writing an EE on pandemic inflation using regional CPI data" — see [[Extended Essay (Economics)]]).

He then cycles through topics for the **international** commentary:

1. **Trump second-term China tariffs** — abandoned. Notably, ChatGPT pushes back hard against fabricating 2025–2026 tariff policy ("I cannot fabricate 2025–2026 trade war details... your IA examiner would eat you alive if you cited imaginary policy"), then web-searches and surfaces the real (post-cutoff) sequence: a 10% blanket import tariff (Apr 2025), Section 232 metal tariffs, a Feb 2026 Supreme Court ruling striking down IEEPA-based tariffs, and the China deficit falling ~32% in 2025. Traveler rejects it because he wants "something that has already happened" with clean effects.
2. **USMCA** — briefly pursued (auto rules of origin, 75% North American content, the 2026 ITC review) but dropped as too diffuse.
3. **Argentina's peso under Milei** — the winner.

## The actual IA: Milei, the crawling peg, and reduced intervention

The essay is built around a **Financial Times article** (the central source, cited repeatedly) reporting that Argentina's central bank, from Jan 2026, would widen the peso's exchange-rate band each month by the prior month's inflation rate rather than a fixed 1%, plus launch a reserve-buying programme (target $10bn by Dec 2026). This is framed against the April 2025 $20bn [[IMF]] deal and Argentina's chronically low dollar reserves and October peso run.

Traveler does real economic reasoning here, and the transcript captures his confusions getting resolved:
- **Forex graph orientation** — he gets genuinely lost over whether to plot ARS/USD or USD/ARS, and why a leftward demand shift lowers price (a multi-turn struggle to make the diagram match his depreciation argument).
- **Real vs nominal depreciation** — works out that the old policy (1% band crawl < 2.5% monthly inflation) caused *real appreciation*, hurting export competitiveness; the new policy allows real depreciation.
- **Demand shift cause** — lands on **consumer/investor expectations** as his named term for why peso demand shifts.
- **GDP / external balance** — reasons through C+I+G+(X−M), why reserves ran short (trade imbalance + debt service + import payments), and is correctly cautioned *not* to overclaim that the old policy caused GDP decline.

He also asks for the historical-context intro (Britannica on 1980s hyperinflation >1000%, the three currency resets 1983–1992), MLA in-text and full citations, and — revealingly — "whats some bs reason i can make up for why i want to study argentina."

## The rubric-engineering payoff

The session ends on the key IB constraint: the commentary must center the **government intervention** key concept. Rather than write a full "increase vs decrease in intervention" comparison (no word budget), he's steered to a question that satisfies the rubric while keeping focus on effects:

> *To what extent does government intervention in the foreign exchange market affect Argentina's external balance?*

— with a single framing line ("a shift toward reduced government intervention... allowing the peso to depreciate in line with inflation") doing the rubric work.
