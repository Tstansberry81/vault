# Oklahoma NFL QBs, the STEP Prayer Study, and Tiger vs Scheffler

A short, casual ChatGPT session from January 2026 that bounces across three topics: which Oklahoma Sooners quarterbacks are active in the NFL, the famous hospital intercessory-prayer study, and a sustained sports-debate about whether Scottie Scheffler is "close" to Tiger Woods. Mostly leisure trivia, but the middle tangent and the golf argument both reveal recognizable strands of [[Traveler Stansberry]]'s thinking.

## Oklahoma QBs
Quick factual ask. The answer lists five OU-alumni QBs on 2025-26 NFL rosters: Baker Mayfield (Buccaneers), Kyler Murray (Cardinals), Jalen Hurts (Eagles), Spencer Rattler (Saints), and Dillon Gabriel (Browns). No analysis — pure curiosity.

## The STEP prayer study
He pivots to "hospital prayer study," prompting a breakdown of the **STEP** (Study of the Therapeutic Effects of Intercessory Prayer, *American Heart Journal*, 2006): ~1,800 cardiac-bypass patients, three arms (prayed-for-unaware, not-prayed-for, prayed-for-aware). Result: no benefit, and the group that *knew* they were being prayed for did slightly worse — read as a performance-anxiety/psychological effect, not a metaphysical one. Traveler's follow-up ("still interesting tho?") fits his [[Political and Economic Views|skeptical]], determinist temperament: he is drawn to a case where science can measure the body but "meaning slips through the cracks." The model explicitly frames it as "perfect TOK bait" — faith vs. reason as ways of knowing, the limits of randomized trials on non-physical claims — which ties directly to [[Theory of Knowledge]] and his recurring [[Faith vs. Knowledge]] interest as a [[Political and Economic Views|religious skeptic]]. The takeaway he'd endorse: the study disproves bad reasoning on *both* sides, not religion itself.

## Tiger vs. Scheffler
The longest thread. Traveler pushes a genuine sports-analytics argument: modern players are better trained, swing harder, and the skill *floor* keeps rising, so dominance should be read relative to era. The conversation lands on a precise framing he accepts — the average tour pro today is meaningfully better (club speed ~114-115 mph and driving distance ~300 yds now vs. ~110 mph / ~285 yds in early-Tiger years), yet Tiger's *margin over his peers* (20 PGA wins in 45 starts vs. Scheffler's ~130) remains unmatched. His instinct to separate "deeper field" from "larger margin," and to reject the lazy leap that a stronger field makes Tiger less impressive, mirrors the quantitative, distinction-drawing habit of mind documented elsewhere in his [[Intellectual Profile]] and his interest in [[UVA and the Quant Question|stats and modeling]].
