# Compact History Midterm Review - Pre-Columbus to the Civil War

A December 2023 study-organization chat from Traveler's sophomore-year US History course at [[St. Paul's School]], roughly two months before [[Gilded Age Flashcards - Populist Era Terms and Labor Strikes (chat)|the Gilded Age flashcard session]] and well before the [[IB History (HL)]] sequence began. He pastes an enormous, messily formatted midterm review sheet and asks the model to "compact this information into bullet points" — purely a cleanup-and-formatting task with no analysis requested.

The review sheet itself is the substance: a chronological term bank running from Pre-Columbus (Cahokia, the Iroquois Confederacy, the Aztec/Maya/Inca empires) through Spanish, French, and Dutch colonization, Puritan New England (the Elect, predestination, the Mayflower Compact, "City Upon a Hill"), the mercantilist and slave-trade systems (Navigation Acts, Triangular Trade, the Middle Passage), the Revolution (the taxation crises, Lexington and Concord, Saratoga, Yorktown, the two Treaties of Paris), the Constitutional founding (the Virginia and New Jersey Plans, the Great Compromise, the three-fifths compromise, the Federalist Papers), the Federalist 1790s and Jefferson's election of 1800, the Market Revolution and Age of Jackson (the American System, the Bank War, the Trail of Tears, the Nullification Crisis), and the long road to the Civil War (Manifest Destiny, the Mexican War, abolitionism, the Compromise of 1850, Bleeding Kansas, Dred Scott, John Brown at Harpers Ferry). The instructor's framing is explicit — group terms geographically, politically, socially, or economically to prepare thesis statements — which is the analytical move Traveler would later master in [[IB History (HL)|HL History]] essays and the [[Theory of Knowledge|TOK]] historiography work.

No images, no model commentary, nothing to flag as contradicting existing wiki claims; the assistant simply reformats the list (delivered across two messages because of its length). The page's value is documentary: it captures the full content map of his early American-history grounding — much of it directly relevant to texts on his [[Reading List]] like [[Narrative of the Life of Frederick Douglass]] (Douglass and the Underground Railroad appear in the term bank) and the slavery-and-abolition material underpinning [[The Moral Impact of Slavery]] — and it shows the same instinct for turning raw class material into structured study tools that runs through his work all the way to [[Homework Hatch (startup)]].
