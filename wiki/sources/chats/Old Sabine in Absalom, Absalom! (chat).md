# Old Sabine in Absalom, Absalom! (chat)

A reading-comprehension session in which Traveler quizzes ChatGPT through the dense, fragmented back-half of [[Absalom, Absalom!]] for his [[English Curriculum 2022-2026]] coursework. The questions are clearly tracking a live reading (he asks specifically to "summarize pages 235 to 243"), and they cluster around the novel's central genealogical mystery — the lineage of Charles Bon — rather than thematic abstraction. This is Traveler using the model as a plot-and-character untangler for one of Faulkner's most notoriously difficult texts.

The thread walks through:
- **Old Sabine / Eulalia Bon's family** — the New Orleans Creole connection that arranged Thomas Sutpen's first marriage.
- **Charles Bon's parentage** — that Bon is Sutpen's son by his abandoned first wife, making him the half-brother *and* rival of Henry Sutpen for Judith.
- **Sutpen's abandonment** — Sutpen voids the marriage on discovering Eulalia's Black ancestry, the act that detonates his "design."
- **Bon's marriage to the octoroon woman** and **Eulalia's role** — read as defiance, search for identity, and tragic irony.
- **The lawyer(s)** — both the one who handles Sutpen's divorce (the legal system as complicit in Southern racial hierarchy) and the intermediary who carries Bon's letters to Judith.
- **Narration of Chapter 8** — the collaborative reconstruction by Quentin Compson and his Harvard roommate Shreve.

What it reveals: Faulkner's whole architecture is about **how history is constructed from fragments, memory, and speculation** — Quentin and Shreve "piecing together" a story riddled with gaps. That maps directly onto Traveler's recurring interests in [[Memory and Trauma]] and [[Identity and Its Collapse]], and the novel's engine of racial "purity," inheritance, and ruthless ambition connects to [[The Moral Impact of Slavery]] and [[Power, Hierarchy, and Justice]]. Sutpen's rigid "design" destroying his own family is a clean instance of [[Individual vs. Society]] and [[Fate and Free Will]].

> [!note] Caveat
> The model's page-235-243 summary is hedged ("typically fall within," "often include") and one answer references a non-existent "Sabine Bercée" surname — ChatGPT is confabulating specifics here. Useful as scaffolding for [[Absalom, Absalom!]], not as a citable authority.
