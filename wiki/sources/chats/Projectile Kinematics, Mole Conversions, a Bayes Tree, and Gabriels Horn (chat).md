# Projectile Kinematics, Mole Conversions, a Bayes Tree, and Gabriel's Horn

A grab-bag homework session for [[Traveler Stansberry]] that starts squarely in [[IB Physics (HL)]] and then wanders across chemistry, statistics, and calculus. He opens with "kinematics with horizontal and vertical motion" and gets the core projectile-motion lesson: horizontal and vertical components are independent ($v_x=v\cos\theta$, $v_y=v\sin\theta$), with the standard time-of-flight, max-height, and range formulas. He then asks for a ramped set of practice problems (table launches, dropped rocks, cliff kicks, angled launches) with worked answers — the kind of drill that recurs across his physics study chats.

The thread then drifts: he asks about **mole / molar mass / atomic mass unit conversions** (Avogadro's number as the "middleman," the $1\text{ u}=1.66\times10^{-24}\text{ g}$ bridge, and what the non-standard "g/u" he'd written would even mean), suggesting cross-subject homework crammed into one session. Next he uploads an image of a drunk-driver detention problem, which the model strips down to a **Bayes' theorem / conditional probability** exercise — building a full probability tree and surfacing the base-rate effect (only ~35% of detained drivers are actually drunk because 88% of the population isn't). This overlaps directly with the probability/stats material he flagged as his weak spot in his [[IB Math SL Paper 1 and 2 Walkthrough - Binomial, Series, and TI-84 Drills (chat)|IB Math SL exam prep]].

The most revealing stretch is calculus he hadn't been taught yet: he asks how to find the surface area of $y=1/x$ rotated about the $y$-axis, and when told it involves arc length and surface-of-revolution integrals, says "i haven't learned any of that stuff yet so teach em." He works through $ds=\sqrt{1+(dy/dx)^2}\,dx$, the **Gabriel's Horn paradox** (finite volume, infinite surface area), and sphere volume by disk integration — well beyond SL scope, fitting his pattern of reaching past the syllabus toward the math that matters for the [[UVA and the Quant Question|quant path]]. The chat closes on a tangent about Virginia's **2026 congressional gerrymandering map** ("packing vs cracking," the "lobster district"), which touches his [[Political and Economic Views]].
