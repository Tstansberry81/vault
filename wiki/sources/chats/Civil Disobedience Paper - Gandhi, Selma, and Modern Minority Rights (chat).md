# Civil Disobedience Paper - Gandhi, Selma, and Modern Minority Rights (chat)

A start-to-finish ghostwriting/research session for a formal [[IB History (HL)]] paper (HL History 2, Mr. Shipley, due Feb 27 2026) on civil disobedience. The prompt asked Traveler to synthesize Thoreau's *Civil Disobedience* and the 1982 film *Gandhi*, then answer: what civil disobedience is and how it works, how it worked against Britain, how Black Americans used it, and whether it can fix problems today (both U.S. minority groups and global issues). He had watched all of *Gandhi* with notes but admitted he had "not really touched" Thoreau. Over the session he used ChatGPT to gather quotes, history, examples, APA formatting, citations, and a final critique — then pasted the finished ~2,000-word paper for grading. This is one of the clearest windows into how Traveler actually produces [[Academic Record|coursework]]: heavy AI scaffolding, then his own synthesis and voice on top.

## What he extracted
- **Theory (Thoreau):** definition as nonviolent, public, deliberate law-breaking with acceptance of punishment; key quotes ("that government is best which governs least"; the "counter-friction to stop the machine" passage; "sitting upon another man's shoulders") that he wove directly into the paper.
- **Gandhi:** South Africa as the "training arc" (Pietermaritzburg train, 1908 pass-burning, mass strikes, accepting imprisonment, the development of *Satyagraha*); India (Non-Cooperation, the 1930 Salt March, hunger strikes, Quit India). He pinned dates — Salt March Mar 12–Apr 6 1930, independence Aug 15 1947 — and the multi-cause analysis of why Britain left (WWII exhaustion, civil disobedience, INA/Navy mutiny, Attlee/Labour, partition).
- **U.S. Civil Rights:** Montgomery Bus Boycott, Greensboro sit-ins, Freedom Rides, Birmingham, and especially Selma/Bloody Sunday (Mar 7 1965 → Voting Rights Act). He cited MLK's *Letter from Birmingham Jail*.
- **Modern application:** he chose two U.S. minority cases — transgender rights (post-Trump 2025 executive orders) and book bans (PEN America's *Beyond the Shelves*) — plus, when the rubric demanded a global lens, authoritarian-vs-democratic comparison (Iran, Hong Kong, apartheid South Africa, India's farmers' protests).

## APA mechanics
A large chunk is pure formatting labor: he had ChatGPT build an APA 7 mock title-page/template .docx, explain in-text page vs. paragraph vs. timestamp citations, the `et al.` rules, and assemble an alphabetized references list. He fed in raw tracking-laden URLs and the real author names (Rummler & Sosin for The 19th; Meehan/Baêta et al. for PEN America) to correct organization-as-author entries.

## What it reveals
- His working method: outsource breadth and formatting, but the final paper is genuinely his — the Hitler-as-book-banner jab, the "I wrote 2k to piss him off" spite over a 1k minimum, and his own argument that social media makes today "the best time in history" for civil disobedience.
- His instincts run toward the provocative and the determinist/[[Individual vs. Society|individualist]]: he drafted a conclusion claiming that under authoritarian rule "militancy becomes the only feasible path to equality," and ChatGPT pushed back hard that this reads as *endorsing* violence rather than analyzing structural constraints. This fits his [[Political and Economic Views|skeptical, contrarian streak]] and connects to themes in [[Civil Disobedience]], [[Self-Reliance]], and [[Power, Hierarchy, and Justice]].
- The graded critique (8/10 content, "easily an A with polishing") flagged real weaknesses: overwritten definition, asserting rather than analyzing the trans section, the Nazi analogy, and the sloppy "(Stanford)" citation.
