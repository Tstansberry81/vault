# Narrow Road Identity Essay - Roles vs Ideology and the IB Rubric Climb

A working session on Traveler's [[IB History (HL)|HL]] [[English Curriculum 2022-2026|English]] comparative essay on [[The Narrow Road to the Deep North]] for Mr. Mezeske (dated 3 December 2025). The prompt: *"Compare and contrast the ways in which Richard Flanagan uses identity to illustrate the conflict of fate and free will."* He pastes a full draft, asks for an IB HL rubric grade, then drives an iterative, section-by-section revision focused almost entirely on the **thesis**. This is one of the clearer windows into how he actually builds an essay: thesis-first, concept-spine before prose.

## The argument
His core claim engages [[Fate and Free Will]] directly through [[Identity and Its Collapse|identity]]: Flanagan presents identity not as a fixed inner self but as **roles people adopt or submit to** under extreme pressure. **Dorrigo Evans** consciously adopts situational, temporary roles — lover (Amy), the "Big Fella" leader in the POW camp, husband/father (Ella), reflective old man — and this belief in choice sustains him to a peaceful death ("At the age of ninety-four, he was finally a free man"). **Nakamura** instead submits to the totalizing, collective "Japanese spirit," an imposed ideological role that erases individual agency and leads to moral collapse (beating his wife and children). The thesis the assistant pushes him toward: not "free will good, fate bad," but that *believing* you choose your role is psychologically sustaining even if free will is illusory — agency constructed inside fate. This maps tightly onto Traveler's own [[Political and Economic Views|determinism]]: he is arguing the *perception* of free will matters, not its metaphysical reality, which is a notably sophisticated position for him to land on.

## The rubric mechanics
Both drafts score **15/20** (A:4, B:3, C:4, D:4) — a "strong 5 / low 6." The diagnosed bottleneck is **Criterion B (Analysis)**: he explains *character psychology* and renders *moral judgments* ("better person," "good man," "bad man") instead of analyzing **authorial construction** (narration, imagery, retrospective structure, collective-vs-individual language). The repeated fix: replace "this shows / proves / symbolizes" and moral labels with "Flanagan constructs / the narrative frames / the retrospective perspective allows," and treat characters as **agents vs vessels**, not good vs bad. This is the same craft-over-summary gap flagged across his other essay-coaching chats (see [[Death of a Salesman Essay - IB HL Criterion Feedback on Fatherhood and Identity (chat)|Death of a Salesman Essay - IB HL Criterion Feedback on Fatherhood and Identity]] and [[Tangent and Normal Line Problems, then Narrow Road Essay Grading (chat)|Tangent and Normal Line Problems, then Narrow Road Essay Grading]]).

## Thesis evolution
The back-half of the chat is pure thesis iteration. He worries the statement is too complex, wants to keep fate/free will as the guiding axis, and tests framings ("roles vs core values" — rejected as too soft and morally neutral, since it accidentally softens Nakamura). He lands on:

> "Flanagan uses identity in the novel to contrast how identity can be composed, either by role adaptation or by rigid ideological construction, arguing that the perception of free will is more beneficial for identity than acceptance of fate."

The key conceptual move he surfaces himself: distinguishing **two types of role adoption** — chosen/situational/self-authored (Dorrigo) vs imposed/collective/ideological (Nakamura). The assistant suggests "more sustaining" over "more beneficial" to avoid circular phrasing.

## What it reveals
Traveler workshops by interrogating his own argument ("what does that mean though and does my essay support it") rather than accepting rewrites — he explicitly refuses full rewrites and insists on "editing each part independently." He understands the conformity/ideology theme well (the "Japanese spirit" as [[Conformity and Tradition]] and [[Individual vs. Society]] erasing agency) and instinctively reads the novel through control and survival. The session connects his coursework directly to his own [[Core Convictions|convictions]] about [[Fate and Free Will]].
