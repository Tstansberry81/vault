# Rational Function Limits and a Trig-Derivatives Midterm Cram

A December 2025 math help session for [[IB Math (SL)]] in which Traveler works through limits of rational functions and then pivots into a broad cram sheet for an upcoming midterm. The conversation is notable mostly as a window into where he was in the calculus/trig sequence senior year, and into how he uses ChatGPT (a deliberately abrasive, roast-heavy tutor persona) to clean up genuine conceptual confusion.

## What he was working on
The opening question — a bare three-word prompt, "limits for rational function" — gets a crash course covering finite-point limits (plug in; vertical asymptote when the denominator hits zero; the 0/0 "boss fight" resolved by factoring, rationalizing, or L'Hôpital) and end-behavior limits as x → ∞ (degree comparison: n < m → 0, n = m → ratio of leading coefficients, n > m → blows up).

The revealing moment is his **conceptual error**: he asserts that "horizontal asymptote is what x approaches, vertical is what y approaches but cant touch" — exactly backwards. The model corrects him firmly (horizontal = a y = constant line as x → ±∞; vertical = an x = constant line the y-value blows up at). This is the kind of axis-confusion that shows he was still solidifying the material rather than reviewing mastered content.

## The midterm scope
He then lays out his full midterm syllabus, which doubles as a snapshot of the course at that point:
- **Unit circle trig** in radians (key values, ASTC quadrant signs)
- **Trig identities** (Pythagorean, sum/difference, double-angle, reciprocal, cofunction)
- **Trig rules for sides** — Law of Sines, Law of Cosines, and triangle area ½·ab·sin C in radians
- **Derivatives** — power, chain, product, quotient rules; end behavior; second derivatives (concavity, inflection); equations of tangent and normal lines

The final exchange drills the Law of Sines (including the cursed SSA ambiguous case — 0, 1, or 2 triangles) and the Law of Cosines as a "non-right-triangle Pythagoras," with a when-to-use decision table. He explicitly asks for flashcards and practice problems to follow, signaling an active, test-prep study mode.
