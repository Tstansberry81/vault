# Unemployment Concepts for Econ SL - Discouraged Workers, Beveridge Curve, LFPR

A second-year [[IB Economics (SL)]] study session from September 2025 in which Traveler works through the **unemployment** unit of the IB textbook, with a brief detour into US history (the Great Society). The chat reads as exam-prep note-condensing: he asks for the "stripped-down notes you'd want before a test," then drills into the conceptual edge cases examiners reward.

## Core macro content
- **Types of unemployment**: frictional, structural, cyclical (demand-deficient), seasonal — the standard IB taxonomy.
- **Natural rate** = frictional + structural, shown at full-employment output (Yf) on the [[IB Economics (SL)]] LRAS diagram.
- **Policy fixes**: demand-side (expansionary fiscal/monetary) for cyclical unemployment; supply-side (retraining, education, labor-market flexibility) for structural — with the inflation trade-off near full employment.

## The conceptual edge cases (what he was really after)
- **Discouraged workers** do *not* count as unemployed — they've stopped actively seeking, so they fall out of both the numerator and the labor force entirely. The "statistical gaslighting" result: the official rate can *fall* even as fewer people hold jobs.
- **Hidden unemployment** = discouraged workers + underemployment + informal economy; the magic evaluation phrase is that the official rate "understates economic hardship."
- **Labor Force Participation Rate (LFPR)** = labor force / working-age population — flagged as an "evaluation weapon" for any "limitations of the unemployment rate" question.

## Real-world data layer
He asks for **FRED job-openings-to-unemployment graphs** and the **Beveridge Curve**. Key takeaway: the openings-to-unemployed ratio peaked ~2.0 in March 2022 (a very tight labor market), then normalized toward the ~1.2 pre-pandemic level. This mirrors the labor-market-tightness framing in his [[Extended Essay (Economics)]] (Maryland pandemic labor market). A small but telling moment: he interprets the inverse ratio (unemployed ÷ openings) and gets the *direction* right but says "people wanting them"; ChatGPT nitpicks that "wanting" is sloppy because discouraged/underemployed workers muddy it — he pushes back ("thats exactly what i said lmao"), showing he tracks the economics even when the phrasing is loose.
