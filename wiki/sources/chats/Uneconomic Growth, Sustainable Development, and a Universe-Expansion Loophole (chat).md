# Uneconomic Growth, Sustainable Development, and a Universe-Expansion Loophole

A combative, idea-testing session for [[IB Economics (SL)]] in which [[Traveler Stansberry]] repeatedly tries to dismantle the textbook concept of sustainable development and gets pushed back each time. He opens by half-defining uneconomic growth (Herman Daly's idea that growth's costs can exceed its benefits — net-negative welfare even before resources run out) and then floats a deliberately provocative claim: that "in an economic sense, we have infinite resources and we don't have to sustain life indefinitely." ChatGPT corrects him hard, anchoring on scarcity as the founding axiom of economics and distinguishing relative from absolute decoupling.

The most characteristic moment is Traveler arguing his textbook's sustainable-development definition is "rendered void in both practical and ideal situations," then escalating to a physics gambit: if the universe is expanding, don't we have a growing finite pool of resources? The reply (space expanding ≠ usable matter increasing; distant galaxies recede past the horizon; entropy still applies) shuts the loophole. This pattern — using a clever cross-disciplinary angle to attack a constraint — fits his determinist-leaning [[Intellectual Interests]] streak.

The conversation then turns genuinely interesting on incentives: Traveler intuits that sustainability "relies on the assumption that everyone has an equal incentive to care about everyone else," and ChatGPT reframes it via the [[Individual vs. Society]] tension — sustainability does NOT assume altruism; it assumes self-interest and tries to align it through carbon pricing, regulation, and internalizing externalities (Tragedy of the Commons). He probes whether sustainable development is a form of communism (overlap in collectivist values, not in system structure) and closes skeptical of the UN's 17 SDGs (zero fully achieved, ~15–20% of targets on track), framing them as a coordination/signaling framework rather than a realistic checklist. The skepticism toward grand collective mission statements echoes his [[Political and Economic Views]] and individualist disposition.

> [!note] What it reveals
> Traveler argues to learn — he advances bold, sometimes wrong positions specifically to see where they break, treating the tutor as a sparring partner. The recurring instinct is to question whether stated constraints and ideals actually bind in reality.
