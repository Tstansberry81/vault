# The One Graded 29/30 - Steinbeck Pastiche, Tangent Lines, and Entrepreneur Odds

A scattershot January 2026 session that braids together three threads from Traveler's life: an [[IB Math (SL)]] calculus review, an entrepreneurship reality-check, and the full submission + grading of his [[IB History (HL)]]-adjacent HL English creative project [[The One]]. The standout is that this chat contains the **complete text** of the story and ends with the actual grade: **29/30**.

## Calculus warm-up (IB Math SL)
He opens with trivia (Wyoming vs. Maryland land/population — his home state of Maryland is "tiny and packed") then pivots to derivative drills tied to [[The One]]'s author's own coursework. Topics: differentiating (1−2x)/x² three ways (power-rule split, quotient rule, and — at his insistence — the chain rule, "a chainsaw to butter bread"); the general chain rule; d/dx of e^(3x−1) = 3e^(3x−1) evaluated at x=0 giving 3/e; finding tangent and **normal lines** to y=√(3x+4) at (4,4); and the full menu of line-equation forms (point-slope, slope-intercept, standard, two-point, intercept). Routine [[IB Math (SL)]] differentiation and analytic-geometry practice — consistent with his other calculus chats (Product/Quotient/Chain Rule drills, Tangent Lines and Extrema review).

## Entrepreneurship odds — research that fed the story
He asks what percent of entrepreneurs ever personally clear $1M+: the answer lands at **~1–3%, most credibly ~1%**, with the gap explained by revenue ≠ personal income, equity dilution (founders often end with 5–15% at exit), survivorship bias, and industry (VC-backed tech ~2–5%). This statistic is not idle — it becomes the thesis of his story's abstract, reflecting his own ambivalence about the [[Homework Hatch (startup)]] grind and the AI-millionaire mythology he distrusts. Connects to his [[Political and Economic Views]] and [[Intellectual Interests]].

## "The One" — full submission and grading
The core of the chat: he pastes the complete four-chapter short story [[The One]] plus its abstract, written for Mr. Mezeske's HL English 2 modern-allegory assignment reinterpreting Steinbeck's *[[The Pearl]]*. Jason (an MIT comp-sci prodigy from Hagerstown, MD on full merit) grows jealous of his roommate Frank's AI virtual-assistant idea, tries to steal it, and ultimately **murders Frank**, only to watch OpenAI ship the same product while he rots in prison reading *The Pearl*. The allegory maps cleanly: Jason = Kino, "The One" = the pearl, Frank = Juana/moral conscience, Big Tech/VC = the rigged pearl market. Each chapter opens with a *Pearl* epigraph.

ChatGPT grades it twice — first against the rubric criterion-by-criterion (strong allegory and contemporary connection; the weakness is the **murder being too abrupt and psychologically compressed**, and Frank being underdeveloped vs. Steinbeck's actively-resisting Juana; "earn it, don't shortcut it"), then recalibrates after Traveler notes it's a **grade-booster** assignment graded generously. Estimate climbed from ~22–24/30 to 25–28/30.

> **Outcome:** Traveler reports back: **"i got a 29."**

This shows him using AI as an IB examiner-simulator — extracting criterion-language feedback and a predicted band before submission — a habit visible across his English coaching chats (Death of a Salesman essay feedback, etc.). It also reveals the autobiographical seam in his fiction: the failed-AI-founder protagonist is a darkened mirror of his own [[Homework Hatch (startup)]] ambitions, and the abstract's "the system is designed to prohibit individuals... from entrepreneurship" channels his [[Political and Economic Views]].
