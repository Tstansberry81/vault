# Rotational Equilibrium and Angular Momentum - IB Physics Statics Problems

A focused [[IB Physics (HL)]] homework session (titled "Mass calculation method") in which Traveler works through three rotational-mechanics problems, repeatedly asking ChatGPT to explain things **intuitively** rather than with formulas. The recurring framing — "how do you solve this intuitively," "explain like I know nothing about torque or inertia," "im so confused" — shows him deliberately trading symbol-pushing for physical intuition, his characteristic learning move.

## Problems solved

1. **Balanced bar on a pyramidal pivot.** A 64 cm uniform bar pivots 24 cm from the left, with a 250 g mass on the left end and 120 g on the right. Taking moments about the pivot — with the bar's own weight acting at its 32 cm midpoint (8 cm right of pivot) — gives 250(24) = 120(40) + m(8), so the bar's mass is **150 g**. Intuitive takeaway: the pivot sits closer to the heavier side, so the bar's weight must load the longer, lighter side to balance.

2. **Ladder against a frictionless wall.** A 5.0 kg, 2.5 m ladder at 70° to the floor. Because the wall is frictionless, floor friction alone resists sliding. Moments about the base give the wall reaction R_w = (mg/2)·cot 70° ≈ **8.9 N**, which equals the floor friction. Intuitive check: a steep ladder needs little grip; a flatter one would need much more. He then asked for an even simpler "broom in the corner" explanation stripped of torque/inertia jargon.

3. **Disc + dropped mass (angular momentum conservation).** A 32 g, 2.2 cm disc spinning at 1.3 rad/s has a 4.0 g mass dropped 1.8 cm from center. With no external torque, I₁ω₁ = I₂ω₂ using I_disc = ½MR² and I_mass = mr². Adding rotational inertia slows it to **1.1 rad/s** — the figure-skater-arms-out analogy.
