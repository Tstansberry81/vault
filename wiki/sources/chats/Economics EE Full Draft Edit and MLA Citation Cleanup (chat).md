# Economics EE Full Draft Edit and MLA Citation Cleanup

A long (319 KB) working session in which Traveler pasted his entire [[Extended Essay (Economics)]] draft into ChatGPT for line-level rating, structural critique, and — for most of the conversation — meticulous MLA citation construction and source verification. It is one of the most complete windows in the vault into the actual writing and finishing of his EE.

## The essay
The research question is fixed throughout: **"To what extent did the Coronavirus pandemic's reactionary fiscal policy such as the CARES Act and the American Rescue Plan Act affect inflation in Maryland during 2020-2023."** This is the same project documented in [[Economics EE Four Arguments and BLS-FRED Data Sourcing (chat)|Economics EE Four Arguments and BLS-FRED Data Sourcing]], [[Economics EE Examiner Feedback and AD-AS Graph (chat)|Economics EE Examiner Feedback and AD-AS Graph]], and the [[EE Submission Tips - Maryland Labor Market Tightness and Federal Debt (chat)|EE Submission Tips - Maryland Labor Market Tightness and Federal Debt]] chats, here seen at near-final draft stage. The argument: ~$4 trillion in federal stimulus (CARES + ARPA) plus Maryland's RELIEF Act drove the June 2022 inflation peak (>9% national, 6.3% Baltimore-Columbia-Towson CPI-U), operating through four channels — demand-pull stimulus, debt-fueled inflation, near-zero interest rates / energy cost-push (Russia-Ukraine, Brent crude, LNG), and labor-market tightness (wage-price spiral, falling LPR). The Maryland scope keeps it within IB's "narrow focus" expectation, a recurring theme in his [[IB Economics (SL)]] work.

## What he was doing
- **Rating and structural edits.** ChatGPT scored the draft a Level 4 (13-16/34) with a path to Level 5-6, flagging repetition (the inflation-peak sentence appears ~4×), run-on "laundry-list" sentences, sermonizing conclusion ("History repeats itself"), and unbuilt graph placeholders ("Graph:" / "Explain Graph:"). He learned the EE rubric: A Focus/Method (6), B Knowledge (6), C Critical Thinking (12), D Presentation (4), E Engagement (6) = 34 total, mapped to A-E letter bands.
- **Formatting logistics.** Whether an RPPF is required (yes, and it doesn't count toward word count), running headers, title-page rules, and target word count — he notes his draft is only ~3300 of the 4000-word limit.
- **Citation grind (the bulk).** Dozens of turns converting raw data links (FRED series MDURN, LBSSA24, JTSJOL; BLS LASST24; Treasury "Debt to the Penny"; CBO; NBER; MIT Sloan's Kritzman finding that 42% of inflation traced to federal spending) into clean MLA Works Cited entries, fixing alphabetization, stripping `utm_source=chatgpt.com` tails from URLs, and verifying figures (the $23.2T → $31.4T federal debt growth, the $1,246/month Maryland household cost).

## What it reveals
- **Source integrity instinct.** Repeatedly he pushes back to keep the work honest: *"look up if that data is correct and sources for it i didnt hallunicate it im not an ai,"* and refuses a citation URL that ends in `chatgpt.com` because it exposes AI use and breaks. He uploads screenshots of a NYTimes 404 to debug a dead source rather than fabricate one. This is the same evidence-grounded, data-driven economics persona seen across his [[Political and Economic Views]] and supply-side leanings.
- **Iterative, granular workflow.** He doesn't ask for a rewrite — he feeds the essay paragraph by paragraph, asking targeted questions ("is that 23.2 trill figure right," "do i cite this anywhere," "national unemployment rate compared to maryland"). It documents how he uses the model as an editor and citation engine, not a ghostwriter — relevant context for the academic-honesty tensions his own startup [[Homework Hatch (startup)]] navigates.
