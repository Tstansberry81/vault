# Rotational Motion Crash Course - Torque, Rolling Without Slipping, and a Bombed HL Test

A long, image-heavy [[IB Physics (HL)]] tutoring session in which Traveler works through the rotational dynamics unit at [[St. Paul's School]], starting from a worksheet of old AP Physics problems and ending — revealingly — with him reporting that he "fucking bombed" the HL test on this exact material. The conversation is one of his most complete records of struggling-then-mastering a hard physics topic.

## What he was doing
He fed ChatGPT photographed multiple-choice problems (a pivoted rod with point masses 3M₀ and M₀, a figure-skater angular-momentum question, a bowling-ball rolling-without-slipping climb) and pushed back when answers were wrong. Notably he **caught the model's error**: ChatGPT first computed the pivoted-rod problem as `5g/7ℓ` by wrongly treating both torques as same-direction, and Traveler corrected it against his teacher's answer key — the right answer is `g/7ℓ` because the pivot sits *between* the masses, so the torques oppose. This is a good signal of him reasoning independently rather than accepting AI output.

From there he asked progressively foundational questions — "what does without slipping mean," "what is translational speed," whether gravity torque "acts at the center of mass" — showing he was rebuilding the conceptual base, not just hunting answers. He requested a full unit overview, then two batches of medium-difficulty practice problems with worked solutions (energy on inclines, pulley-and-block systems, turntable angular-momentum conservation, work-energy checks).

## Key physics content he extracted
- The "type-the-problem-first" strategy: **pivot → torque (`Στ = Iα`); rolling → energy + `v = ωR`; no external torque → `L = Iω` conserved.**
- Moment-of-inertia table (solid cylinder ½MR², sphere ⅖MR², hoop MR², rod-about-end ⅓ML²) and the parallel-axis theorem.
- Rolling without slipping locks `v = ωR` / `a = αR`, static friction does no work, so energy conservation holds.
- A common trap he personally hit: putting the incline angle θ into the *torque* arm. The torque from friction on a cylinder is `fR` (friction ⊥ radius), while θ belongs only in the *force* decomposition `Mg sinθ`. Deriving `a = ⅔g sinθ` for a solid cylinder cemented this.

## The hard problem and the test
The final third is him wrestling with a compound rigid-body system — a wheel on an axle with a rod and end mass, a string attached tangentially holding it in rotational equilibrium, then "the string is cut" and you solve for initial angular acceleration and later the linear velocity when the rod passes horizontal. He repeatedly reset and re-specified the geometry, untangling which masses actually produce torque (only off-pivot ones) and combining inertia terms.

He then revealed the payoff: this was the exact "solid disk rod thing" that appeared as a short-answer on his HL test, which he bombed. His teacher predicted a ~60 class average. ChatGPT reframed the low score as normal for HL rotational mechanics and praised him for rebuilding the framework afterward rather than walking away.
