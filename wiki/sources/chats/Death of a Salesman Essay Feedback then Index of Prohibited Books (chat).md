# Death of a Salesman Essay Feedback then Index of Prohibited Books

A mixed-coursework session (GPT-5, Oct 2025) that begins with Traveler submitting a full literary essay on [[Death of a Salesman]] for IB-style critique, then drifts into history-research sourcing on the Catholic *Index of Prohibited Books* and a quick logistics check on [[Theory of Knowledge|TOK]] exhibition word counts.

## The essay
Traveler pastes a complete essay arguing that the [[character development]] of Willy and Biff Loman shows that "the identity of men in America is closely tied to their ethical values, idea of success, and work ethic," and that the **collapse of [[Identity and Its Collapse|identity]] is aligned with the role of father figures.** The piece leans hard on his recurring [[Masculinity]] theme: Willy inherits a false manhood from his brother Ben (rich quick, "be liked and you will never want"), has no father of his own, and passes the rot down to Biff — theft, deceit, a hollow definition of success, and an inability to do honest work. He notes Biff lost a football scholarship **to the University of Virginia** by failing math, an aside that resonates given Traveler is himself [[UVA and the Quant Question|UVA-bound]].

The feedback (estimated ~23/30, "solid Level 6 bordering 7") delivers the central lesson he keeps relearning across his [[English Curriculum 2022-2026|English work]]: **plot = summary, technique = analysis.** The model pushes him toward stagecraft (lighting/music blurring time), symbolism (seeds, stockings, the flute), and diction rather than thematic retelling. Traveler iterates well — he workshops a closing line ("when fathers build their lives on false ideals of manhood, their character flaws can become the inheritance of sons") and accepts a rewrite of the hard-work paragraph that folds in the **seed symbolism** ("Nothing's planted. I don't have a thing in the ground") as a metaphor for Willy's failure to leave a legacy. This shows him actively raising a competent-but-summary-heavy draft toward genuine literary analysis.

## The Index of Prohibited Books
Abruptly he switches to a history/research task: was the *Index Librorum Prohibitorum* actually enforced? He gets the arc (strict 1559–1600 in Italy/Spain via the Inquisition, decaying to symbolic by the Enlightenment, abolished 1966), then does something revealing — he demands **verifiable sources and exact quotes** for the specific claim that enforcement ceased by the 18th century. The model offers Pastore (2015), Fantazzi (2020), and Britannica, but when pressed for an *exact quote* honestly admits it cannot verify one from Pastore. Traveler keeps pushing ("doesn't need to be exact but does it mention it"), refusing to cite a source he can't confirm says what he needs — disciplined citation hygiene for an [[IB History (HL)]] or [[Faith vs. Knowledge|religion/censorship]] paper. He also has it reformat a Britannica citation to match an MLA template from a Finocchiaro article on the Church prohibiting Copernicanism.
