# AI and Ambition - The One (Steinbeck Pastiche Feedback)

Traveler submits a full draft of **[[The One]]**, his creative-writing piece for [[IB History (HL)|IB HL English 2]] (Mr. Mezeske, due 13 Jan 2026), and asks ChatGPT for blunt criticism. The story is a deliberate modern recasting of Steinbeck's **[[The Pearl]]**: each chapter opens with an epigraph quoted from the novel, and the arc maps Kino's destruction onto an MIT computer-science student named Jason whose obsession with an AI startup ("The One") drives him to murder his roommate. This chat is the workshop record behind that finished piece in his [[English Curriculum 2022-2026]].

## What the story is doing
The abstract is unusually polemical for a creative submission: it argues that "the structure of our contemporary society allows tech giants to systematically eradicate competition and erode any sense of innovation," framing Y-Combinator and Sam Altman as profiting off the ambition of the young, and citing the water cost of AI servers. This is the same anti-bloat, anti-bubble critique of the AI gold rush that recurs across his other chats (see the [[Homework Hatch (startup)]] threads and the companion "AI Startup Bloat and The Pearl - Greed Abstract" chat) — here weaponized into fiction. The protagonist Jason is a thinly veiled exaggeration of Traveler's own profile: Hagerstown-Maryland setting, science fairs and model rockets, self-taught discrete math and linear algebra, ADHD reframed as curiosity, the [[Investment Club]]-adjacent obsession with the *Acquired* podcast and founder mythology. The story is a dark inversion of the [[Consumerism and Alienation]] and [[Power, Hierarchy, and Justice]] themes — Frank, the murdered roommate with Wall Street connections, literally *is* the pearl.

## The feedback exchange
ChatGPT's first response is a detailed HL-criterion-style critique: praises the controlled Steinbeck parallel and the framing abstract, but flags that (1) the abstract over-claims *systemic inevitability* the narrative doesn't dramatize, (2) Jason is too self-aware too early, confessing his sin before committing it and undercutting the tragedy, (3) the murder is psychologically rushed and needs "one more failed attempt at restraint," (4) Frank is underdeveloped — "more concept than person" — and needs one contrasting value (AI as service vs. dominance), and (5) the closing "It was fate" is too on-the-nose, telling rather than showing. Mechanical fixes flagged: "computer sciences courses," "He need to find," "he throw his tray," overuse of "ambition."

Traveler's reply is the most revealing part: *"in fairness i am not trying to be steinbeck and i am more so trying to show the teacher directly that i know what im talking about."* He states his goal is **demonstrative competence** — proving to Mr. Mezeske that he grasps theme, parallel, and authorial control — not literary invisibility, and notes he already made Jason more conflicted and expanded Frank's worldview. This is characteristic of his strategic, rubric-aware approach to coursework: he treats the assignment as an assessment of interpretive awareness, not an MFA exercise, and ChatGPT validates the calibration ("you're proving you read Steinbeck with your brain turned on").

## Why it matters
The piece is a direct expression of his [[Political and Economic Views]] and [[Fate and Free Will]] (he is a self-described determinist; ending on "It was fate" is no accident) channeled through the [[Individual vs. Society]] and [[Masculinity]]/ambition material of his [[Reading List]]. It also shows the recursive overlap between his fiction and his real life — the [[UVA and the Quant Question|finance-and-ambition]] tension, the startup-founder fantasy, and his skepticism of the AI bubble all surface in a story he wrote *for an English grade*.
