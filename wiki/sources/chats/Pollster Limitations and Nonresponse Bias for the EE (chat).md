# Pollster Limitations and Nonresponse Bias for the EE

A research/methodology chat in which Traveler hands ChatGPT a [Pew Research Center](https://www.pewresearch.org/methods/2023/04/19/how-public-polling-has-changed-in-the-21st-century/) article ("How Public Polling Has Changed in the 21st Century," April 2023) and asks it to extract the main limitations pollsters face when designing and interpreting polls. The framing — and the model's repeated nudges to "map this to your IB Extended Essay topic (fiscal policy + inflation + local case study)" — ties it directly to his [[Extended Essay (Economics)]] on COVID-era inflation and federal/state fiscal policy. He is plainly mining survey-methodology critiques to harden the methodology/data-limitations section of that paper.

## What he extracted
The conversation distills seven recurring limitations:
- **Changing methods don't guarantee accuracy** — 61% of national pollsters used different methods in 2022 vs. 2016, but newer modes (online, ABS, mixed-mode panels) don't automatically fix bias.
- **Nonresponse bias** — declining response rates mean respondents differ systematically from non-respondents.
- **Coverage / frame issues** — young people, low-SES, and cellphone-only households are chronically under-reached; weighting "isn't wizardry."
- **Mode effects** — phone vs. online respondents behave differently, complicating year-to-year trend comparisons.
- **Opaque design decisions** — Pew admits it couldn't track weighting/post-stratification because pollsters rarely publish them, making interpretation "a trust exercise."
- **Generalizing national polls to states/subgroups** is an external-validity problem.
- **Unrealistic expectations** post-2016/2020 — polls are snapshots, "not prophecy machines."

He then demands **exact quotes for points 2–5**, showing the rigor of someone assembling citable evidence rather than vibes, and finally asks whether **nonresponse bias has actually increased in U.S. polling**. The answer is nuanced: response *rates* have clearly fallen (Northwestern IPR, FiveThirtyEight/Ipsos), but whether nonresponse *bias* has consistently risen is mixed — an SSRS 2024 panel found no strong partisan nonresponse after controlling for demographics, and a 2015 Pew line notes "the mere existence of low response rates doesn't tell us anything about whether or not nonresponse bias exists."
