# The Moral Impact of Slavery

A theme Traveler treats with unusual moral seriousness, spanning grade 10 ([[Narrative of the Life of Frederick Douglass|Douglass]]) and grade 12 ([[Beloved]]).

## Core readings
- **Slavery corrupts the enslaver, not just the enslaved.** His sharpest insight (from [[Psychological and Moral Impact of Slavery]] and [[Quiz 6 Paragraph Questions]]): Mrs. Auld, initially kind, becomes crueler than her husband once forbidden to teach Douglass to read — "good people at heart to do unspeakable things." Slavery as a machine of moral corruption.
- **Literacy as liberation / its suppression as control.** Connects directly to [[Faith vs. Knowledge]] — knowledge threatens illegitimate power.
- **Pathos as method** ([[Use of Pathos in An American Slave]]): Douglass's tears and the analysis of slave songs as expressions of sorrow, not contentment.
- **Slavery's afterlife in [[Beloved]]**: trauma as the thing that shapes (and shatters) identity — see [[Identity and Its Collapse]] and [[Memory and Trauma]].

## Cross-text use
He repeatedly deploys slavery as the **real-world benchmark** for fictional oppression — e.g. comparing the patients of [[One Flew Over the Cuckoo's Nest|the ward]] to slaves in the [[Nest Essay]], and contrasting Douglass's physical violence with the merely *attitudinal* racism of [[The Great Gatsby|Tom Buchanan]] in his [[Midterm Essays|midterm]].
