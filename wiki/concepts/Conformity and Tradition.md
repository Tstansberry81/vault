# Conformity and Tradition

The danger of unexamined inherited behavior — how "we've always done it this way" enables atrocity, and how conformity erases the self.

## Key readings
- **[[The Lottery]]** (Shirley Jackson): tradition as lethal ritual. Traveler reads it as a satire of "the reluctance of people to reject outdated traditions," and links it to **the WWI/WWII draft** and to coercion ("good people can do terrible things if they have to" — [[Mid Term Exam Essay]]). The black box = old traditions; names (Summers, Graves, Delacroix) as charactonyms.
- **[[Maus]]**: coercion under Nazi occupation — Jewish police sending others to Auschwitz "not because they wanted to… because they had to" ([[Trials of Solidarity]]); the "banality of evil."
- **[[Exit Ticket 299-332|The Narrow Road / Choi]]**: conformity to "the Japanese spirit" crushes identity — "a man of no colony."
- **The transcendentalist antidote**: [[Self-Reliance]]'s call to "pave your own path" against the herd.

## Through-line
Conformity is the negative space of Traveler's [[Individual vs. Society]] and [[Faith vs. Knowledge]] themes: the systems that win do so by manufacturing consent, and the cost is borne by the nonconformist (Tessie Hutchinson, McMurphy, Choi).
