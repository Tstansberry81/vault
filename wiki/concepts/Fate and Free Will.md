# Fate and Free Will

[[Traveler Stansberry]]'s signature theme — the question he returns to more than any other across four years.

## His evolving position
- **Grade 11 — hard fatalism.** In the [[Iliad Essay]], he argues "the concept of free will in the story is a fallacy." Fate orchestrates everything; Achilles's knowledge of his fate is what kills Patroclus. Humans "sleep easy at night believing we have free will because we do not know that we don't." Fate even has "an innate hierarchy of priorities."
- **Grade 12 — pragmatic existentialism.** In [[The Narrow Road to the Deep North]] essays, he flips: Flanagan's circle vs. line imagery shows fate and free will are "the same, only perceived differently," but the *belief* in free will "is certainly better than believing there is none at all." Dorrigo (chooses roles, survives at peace) beats Nakamura (surrenders to the fatalistic "Japanese spirit," collapses).

> [!warning] Contradiction / evolution
> The [[Iliad Essay]] (free will is an illusion, full stop) and the [[Narrow Road to the Deep North Response]] (the illusion of free will is what gives life purpose) reach nearly opposite conclusions. This is the clearest evidence of Traveler's intellectual maturation — from teenage determinism to a more livable, chosen existentialism.

## Connected works
- [[The Iliad]] — fate as inescapable destiny
- [[The Narrow Road to the Deep North]] — fate (the line) vs. free will (the circle), identity as the battleground
- [[A Sound of Thunder]] — one wrong step rewrites the world (consequence/determinism)
- [[Do Androids Dream of Electric Sheep?]] — Mercerism vs. the inevitability of technological "evolution"

## The personal resolution
His private notes show he didn't leave this question on the page. One reads: *"strong is using willpower to overcome deterministic or selfish tendencies"* — effectively his lived answer to the Iliad-vs-Narrow-Road tension: determinism is real, and strength is the act of resisting it. This is the philosophical engine behind his [[Self-Discipline and Goals|self-discipline regimens]]. See [[Intellectual Interests]].
