# Masculinity

A recurring axis of [[Identity and Its Collapse]] in [[Traveler Stansberry]]'s reading — usually examined as something **toxic, performed, or imprisoning**.

## Key readings
- **[[Beloved]] — two models of Black masculinity.** Sixo (defiant, decisive, dies singing) vs. Paul D (contemplative, reserved, survives). Traveler argues Morrison endorses Paul D's path: "the path to freedom… requires patience and passivity, rather than rebellion." Separately, he reads Beloved herself as "a form of toxic masculinity" that re-traps Paul D in a slave identity.
- **[[A Streetcar Named Desire]] — masculinity as abuse.** Williams "associates masculinity and heteronormality with bad, abusive men" (Stanley, Mitch), and femininity with submissiveness (Stella). Reads the play as "ahead of its time" on toxic masculinity.
- **[[Home (novel)|Home]] — the Black male experience.** Frank Money's PTSD, "savior complex," and the secret that he shot the Korean girl all tie his masculinity to trauma and over-policing.

## Pattern
Traveler consistently frames masculinity as a **role or performance that costs the man his interior life** — connecting it to [[Identity and Its Collapse]] (the self attached to a role) and to [[Power, Hierarchy, and Justice]] (masculinity as dominance, e.g. [[The Lion in Winter]], [[The Great Gatsby|Tom Buchanan]]).
