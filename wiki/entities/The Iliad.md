# The Iliad

Epic by **Homer** (Emily Wilson translation). Studied in [[English Curriculum 2022-2026|IB HL English 1]] (Dr. Harris, Oct 2024). The foundational text for Traveler's [[Fate and Free Will]] thinking.

## Traveler's reading
[[Iliad Essay|"Death Still Comes: Fate and Free Will in The Iliad"]] argues **free will is "a fallacy."** Achilles's knowledge of his fate drives every choice: refusing Agamemnon's embassy, lending Patroclus his armor (sealing Patroclus's death and his own glory). Bold claims:
- Fate "transcends the authority of gods" — even Thetis can only try to alter it.
- Fate has "an innate hierarchy of priorities" (Achilles's lifespan over Patroclus's).
- We believe in free will only because "we do not know that we don't" — fate keeps the illusion to preserve our dignity.

A short companion exercise ([[5-1]]) ranks the essay's key quotes (glory, "welcome death," "do everything or nothing").

## Significance
The **hard-determinist pole** of his thinking. Compare the softened, pragmatic view three years later in [[The Narrow Road to the Deep North]] — see the contradiction in [[Fate and Free Will]].
